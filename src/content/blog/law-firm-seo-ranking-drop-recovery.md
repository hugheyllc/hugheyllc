# The SEO Graveyard: Why Law Firm Websites Stop Ranking (And How to Resurrect Them)

Your law firm website had solid rankings. Phone was ringing. Then, somewhere between last quarter and this one, the calls dried up and your Google positions quietly collapsed. A **law firm SEO ranking drop** rarely announces itself with fanfare. It just happens, and by the time you notice, you're three pages back and your competitor is scooping up every case you used to get.

What causes it? Usually one of three things: Google updated something, your site accumulated enough technical debt to matter, or your content got stale while everyone else's got sharper. Sometimes all three at once. There's no conspiracy here—just cause and effect. The good news is that a **law firm SEO ranking drop** is diagnosable and recoverable, provided you're willing to look at the actual data instead of guessing.

Let's do the forensics.

---

## Algorithm Updates: The Most Misunderstood Culprit

Google runs core algorithm updates several times a year, plus a constant stream of smaller adjustments. Each one recalibrates how pages are evaluated for quality, authority, and relevance. When a core update hits, some sites go up. Some go down. If you went down, that's a signal—not a penalty.

The distinction matters. A penalty means Google caught you doing something against their guidelines. A ranking drop after a core update means Google reassessed your content against its competitors and found yours wanting. The fix for a penalty is different from the fix for a quality signal.

**How to diagnose it:** Pull your Google Search Console data and look for a drop that correlates with a known update date. Google announces core updates publicly. If your traffic fell the same week an update rolled out, that's your culprit. If the drop was gradual over months, it's probably not update-driven—it's something else.

Core updates tend to hit thin content hard. If your practice area pages are 300-word summaries that could have been written by a paralegal in 20 minutes, that's the problem. Google has gotten increasingly good at distinguishing genuinely useful legal content from placeholder text dressed up with keywords.

---

## Technical Debt: The Slow Killer

Most law firm websites are built, launched, and then largely ignored from a technical standpoint. The firm pays someone to add a blog post occasionally, maybe refreshes the homepage photo every few years, and calls it done. Meanwhile, the site accumulates problems.

**Common technical issues that cause a law firm SEO ranking drop:**

- **Page speed deterioration.** Plugins get added. Images never get compressed. Hosting never gets upgraded. The site that loaded in 2.1 seconds in 2020 now loads in 6.4 seconds. Google cares. Mobile users especially care.
- **Broken internal links.** Pages get deleted or URLs change without proper redirects. You end up with a site full of dead ends that search bots can't navigate cleanly.
- **Crawl budget waste.** Large sites with duplicate content, parameter-driven URLs, or improperly indexed pages force Google to spend its crawl budget on useless content instead of your good pages.
- **Core Web Vitals failures.** Google's page experience signals—Largest Contentful Paint, Cumulative Layout Shift, Interaction to Next Paint—affect rankings directly. A site that shifts around while loading or takes too long to respond gets docked.
- **HTTPS and security issues.** An expired SSL certificate or mixed-content warnings can tank trust signals overnight.

None of these are glamorous. None of them show up obviously to a human visitor casually browsing your site. But they add up, and Google's crawlers notice every one of them. ## Content Rot: The Problem Nobody Wants to Admit

Content rot is what happens when your pages were good enough to rank three years ago and haven't been touched since. The web around them evolved. Competitors published better, more comprehensive content. Your pages didn't move. Relative quality dropped. Rankings followed.

Law firm content has a particular version of this problem. Statutes change. Case law evolves. Procedural rules get updated. A page explaining Florida's statute of limitations for personal injury cases that hasn't been verified since 2021 may be quietly spreading outdated information—which Google's quality evaluators notice, and which can do real damage to a firm's E-E-A-T signals (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness).

**The audit question to ask:** When was each major page last substantively updated—not just touched with a minor edit, but actually reviewed for accuracy, depth, and relevance?

Content rot also shows up in your practice area pages. If those pages exist primarily to capture a keyword rather than to answer the questions a prospective client actually has, they're underperforming. ## Local Signal Erosion

For most law firms, organic rankings and local pack visibility are closely tied. If your Google Business Profile isn't optimized, consistently maintained, and accumulating legitimate reviews, your local presence weakens over time—especially as competitors actively manage theirs.

Local ranking factors include proximity, relevance, and prominence. You can't control proximity. You can control relevance and prominence. That means a complete, accurate, keyword-informed Business Profile; a steady cadence of genuine client reviews; and local citations that are consistent across directories. ## The Recovery Roadmap

Diagnosis first. Recovery without diagnosis is just expensive guessing.

**Step 1: Pull the data.** Google Search Console, Google Analytics, and a crawl tool like Screaming Frog or Sitebulb. Identify when the drop started, which pages dropped, and whether there's a technical pattern.

**Step 2: Fix the technical foundation.** Page speed, broken links, redirect chains, crawl errors. These have to be resolved before content improvements will fully land.

**Step 3: Audit and refresh content.** Identify your highest-traffic pages from 12-18 months ago that have since dropped. Update them substantively—add depth, verify accuracy, improve structure, and ensure they actually answer the questions a prospective client would have.

**Step 4: Address local signals.** If your Google Business Profile has stale information, unanswered reviews, or missing content, fix it. Simultaneously audit your citation consistency across directories.

**Step 5: Build new authority signals.** Recovering from a **law firm SEO ranking drop** isn't just about fixing what broke. It requires demonstrating to Google that your site deserves its position back—through updated content, legitimate backlinks, and consistent engagement signals.
