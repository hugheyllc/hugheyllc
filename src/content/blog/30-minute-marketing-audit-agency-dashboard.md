## The 30-Minute Marketing Audit: What to Pull From Your Agency's Dashboard Right Now

You don't need to fire your agency to audit your law firm's marketing performance. You need to know what numbers matter, where to find them, and what they actually mean.

A **law firm marketing audit checklist** doesn't have to be complicated. Most firms I work with inherit agency relationships where reporting is either too vague or too buried in data to act on. [Clio's Legal Trends Report](https://www.clio.com/resources/legal-trends/) finds the majority of law firms can't accurately identify which marketing channels drive their best clients—a gap that starts with opaque agency reporting. This post gives you the dashboard walkthrough—the one your agency probably skips in their monthly recap—so you can see what's actually working in 30 minutes, no MBA required.

This is a post-hire accountability check, not a pre-hire evaluation. You already have an agency. You want to know if the spend is landing, the leads are real, and the conversions justify the cost.

---

## What a Real Law Firm Marketing Audit Actually Measures

A **marketing audit** answers one question: *Is this money turning into cases?*

Most agency reports measure activity: impressions, clicks, form fills, website traffic. Those are inputs, not outcomes. An audit goes deeper. It connects the dots from ad spend all the way to retained client revenue—or at least to the intake stage where your data ends and your client relationship begins.

The agencies that do this best pull from four sources simultaneously:

1. **GA4** — traffic source, landing page performance, user behavior
2. **Call tracking** — call volume, duration, conversion (did they hire you?)
3. **CPC and ad spend trends** — cost per click, budget burn, seasonal patterns
4. **Intake conversion** — from first contact to signed retainer

If your agency hasn't shown you a report that threads these together, they're either not measuring what matters or they're hoping you won't ask for it. [Google's GA4 documentation](https://support.google.com/analytics/answer/10089681) is the baseline for understanding what accurate traffic attribution should look like.

## The Four Dashboards You Need to Pull Right Now

### 1. GA4: Where Are Your Clients Actually Coming From?

Open Google Analytics 4. Navigate to **Reports > Traffic Sources > User Acquisition**.

Look for:
- **Organic search** — SEO-driven traffic. Track the percentage of total sessions and bounce rate. If it's under 20% of traffic, you're either not ranking or your SEO isn't attracting qualified visitors.
- **Paid search** — Google Ads traffic. Should have a lower bounce rate than organic (meaning the ads are more targeted). If bounce rate exceeds 40%, your landing pages aren't matching intent.
- **Direct** — people typing your URL or clicking from emails, reviews, referrals. This is your brand equity. Firms with strong local presence see 15–25% direct traffic.
- **Referral** — directory sites, other referral sources. If this is rising, you're getting spillover from directory listings. If it's flat or declining, directories aren't pulling weight anymore.

Then click into **Engagement > Conversions** (or your custom conversion events). You should have events set up for:
- Form submissions
- Phone number clicks
- Chat initiations
- "Book consultation" button clicks

Track the conversion rate by source. Paid search should outconvert organic if your ads are well-targeted. If they're not, your agency is burning budget on loose intent match.

**What to ask your agency:** "Why is our organic bounce rate higher than our paid search bounce rate?" If they can't answer, your landing pages need a redesign or your SEO targeting is off.

---

### 2. Call Tracking: Are Calls Actually Converting to Clients?

Log into your call tracking system (CallRail, Infinity, etc.). Pull a report for the last 90 days.

You need three numbers:

**Call volume by source** — How many calls came from Google Ads vs. organic vs. direct vs. referral? Firms that understand their intake often see Google Ads driving 40–50% of call volume, organic 20–30%, and referrals filling the rest.

**Call duration** — Are people hanging up after 30 seconds or staying on for 5+ minutes? Short calls usually mean your intake staff isn't qualifying or the caller isn't actually interested. If your average call duration is under 2 minutes, something's wrong—either the ad isn't pre-qualifying or your intake process is weak.

**Conversion rate** — How many calls resulted in a scheduled consultation or a signed engagement? This is the number that matters. If your agency is sending 100 calls a month but only 12 convert to clients, your cost per client isn't what they're telling you.

**What to ask your agency:** "What's our call-to-client conversion rate, and how does it compare to similar firms in our practice area?" If they don't know, pull the number yourself from your CRM or call tracking system. Then ask: "How are we improving this month-over-month?"

---

### 3. CPC and Ad Spend Trends: Is Your Cost Per Click Creeping Up?

In Google Ads, go to **Campaigns > All Campaigns**. Set the date range to the last 6 months. Export to a spreadsheet.

Look for:
- **Average CPC** — Is it rising? Rising CPC usually means increased competition, lower Quality Score, or both. If your CPC has climbed 15% in three months, your ads need tightening or your landing pages need faster load times. (Most firms see CPC increase 5–10% year-over-year due to competition; anything faster than that is a flag.)
- **Cost per conversion** — Divide total ad spend by conversions (form fills or calls, depending on your tracking). Compare this to your average engagement value. If you're spending $1,500 per qualified lead and each client is worth $5,000 in fees, that's workable. If cost per conversion is $3,000+, you need to scale back spend or tighten targeting.
- **Budget allocation by practice area** — Which practice areas are eating most of your budget? Which are returning the highest conversion rate? Agencies often let budget drift toward high-volume keywords without tracking profitability. You may be overspending on low-margin work.

**What to ask your agency:** "Show me a six-month trend of our CPC and cost per conversion. Where do you see optimization opportunities in the next quarter?"

---

### 4. Intake Conversion: From First Contact to Signed Client

This one lives in your CRM or your call tracking system. You need to know:

- **Lead to consultation scheduled** — Of 100 leads (calls, forms, chats), how many agree to a consultation? Healthy conversion is 50–70% for qualified intake.
- **Consultation to engagement** — Of consultations scheduled, how many sign a retainer? This should be 60–80% for firms with decent intake qualifying. If it's below 50%, your sales process is leaking or your leads aren't actually qualified.
- **Time to first contact** — How fast does your firm respond to a new lead? [Speed-to-contact is the #1 driver of law firm growth](/blog/law-firm-intake-speed/). If you're responding in over 2 hours, you're losing deals. Best-in-class firms respond within 15 minutes.

Pull these numbers from your CRM. If your CRM doesn't track them, you have a bigger problem—you can't optimize what you don't measure.

**What to ask your agency:** "What's our overall lead-to-client conversion rate, and where are we leaking deals?" If they say "that's not our job," remind them that marketing's job ends when an intake-qualified lead walks in the door. What happens after that is your process. But the agency should know the baseline so they can help diagnose whether the leak is in their lead quality or your intake.

## How to Read the Audit in Real Time

Once you have these four dashboards open, here's what a healthy picture looks like:

- **GA4 traffic** is split between organic (25–35%), paid search (30–45%), direct (15–25%), and referral (10–15%). Bounce rate on paid search is lower than organic.
- **Call volume** is rising month-over-month (5–15% growth is typical for growing firms).
- **Call duration** averages 3–5 minutes.
- **Call-to-client conversion** is 10–15% (meaning 10–15 of every 100 calls result in a signed client or scheduled consultation that converts).
- **CPC** is stable or rising slowly (no more than 5–10% per quarter due to seasonal competition).
- **Cost per retained client** is 2–4x your average engagement value. So if you sign $15,000 engagements, you should be comfortable spending $30,000–$60,000 to acquire one client.

If your numbers don't match this picture, you have one of three problems:

1. **Lead quality is poor** — Your agency is bidding too broadly or landing pages aren't pre-qualifying.
2. **Intake is broken** — Your team is losing deals after the lead arrives (this is on you, not the agency).
3. **Attribution is incomplete** — You're not tracking the full customer journey, so you don't know where the real value is coming from.

## The Conversation to Have With Your Agency After This Audit

Once you've pulled the four dashboards, schedule a 30-minute call with your agency. Bring the data.

Ask them to walk you through:

1. **"Here's what I'm seeing in GA4. What are you seeing, and are there opportunities in any of these traffic sources?"** They should be able to articulate a specific optimization for each channel. If they say "looks good," they're not thinking deeply enough.

2. **"Our call-to-client conversion is at X%. What benchmarks are you using, and how do we compare?"** A good agency has benchmarks. They know what healthy looks like for your practice area and can explain where you stand.

3. **"Our CPC has moved from $X to $Y in the last three months. What's driving that, and what's the plan?"** They should explain market conditions, competitive density, or changes they've made. They should have a plan to manage it.

4. **"Walk me through our cost per retained client. Is this sustainable given our client lifetime value?"** This is the number that determines whether marketing is an investment or a drain. Your agency should think in terms of client value, not just lead volume.

If your agency can't answer these questions confidently, they're either not analyzing your account deeply or they don't understand how their work connects to your bottom line. ## Why This Audit Matters Now

In 2026, legal marketing has shifted. Agencies can no longer hide behind vanity metrics like "1,000 impressions" or "50 website visits." [Accountability in law firm marketing means showing how spend translates to cases](/blog/accountability-law-firm-marketing-agency-vs-consultant/), and that's measurable in real time.

This 30-minute audit is your permission slip to ask hard questions. It's also your baseline. Run it monthly. Track trends. If nothing's improving after three months of agency work, you have data to back up a difficult conversation.

The firms that do this quarterly are the ones that catch problems early, renegotiate terms when needed, and know exactly what they're paying for. The firms that don't are the ones that wake up six months later wondering why they're spending $5,000 a month and not seeing cases.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What if my agency won't give me access to the dashboards?

That's a red flag. You should have read-only access to your own Google Ads account, GA4, and call tracking system. If they're gatekeeping this data, ask why. Legitimate agencies are transparent about performance because they're confident in their work. If they refuse access, you may need to [consider switching to an independent consultant](/blog/independent-consultant-vs-agency-accountability/) or at minimum demand a transparent reporting structure as a condition of continued engagement.

### How often should I run this audit?

Monthly is ideal. At minimum, quarterly. This lets you spot trends—a rising CPC, a dropping conversion rate, a shift in traffic sources—before they become real problems. The first-month audit tells you the baseline. The second month shows you if things are improving or declining. The third month confirms a pattern.

### What if my intake conversion is below 10%?

That's usually a process problem, not a marketing problem. Your agency is sending leads; your team isn't converting them. Pull your CRM data and look at response time, intake script quality, and objection handling. Often, firms with low intake conversion have a bottleneck—maybe one person handling all calls, or a CRM that isn't automated. Fix that before blaming the agency for lead quality.

### Should I compare my numbers to other firms' benchmarks?

With caution. Every practice area and every market is different. A personal injury firm in Tampa will have different metrics than a business law firm in St. Petersburg. What matters is tracking your own month-over-month and quarter-over-quarter trends. Are *you* improving? That's what counts.

### What if my cost per retained client is way higher than my client lifetime value?

That's unsustainable. You need to either increase client value (higher retainers, more add-on services), reduce marketing spend (tighten targeting, fire underperforming channels), or improve intake conversion (fix your process). This is the conversation that should happen between you and your agency or consultant immediately.
