# Google Business Profile Optimization for Law Firms: Beyond the Basics

To optimize your law firm's Google Business Profile, you need to go well past filling in your address and picking a category. The firms winning the local pack are actively managing reviews, publishing posts on a consistent schedule, controlling the Q&A section before someone else does, and keeping every signal Google watches pointed in the same direction. As for what to include: your primary practice area category, a keyword-informed business description, every relevant service listed, accurate hours, and photos that don't look like they were taken on a 2011 flip phone. That's the floor. Everything below is how you actually compete.

---

## Why "Just Fill It Out" Advice Is Costing You Cases

Every marketing blog in existence will tell you to "claim your Google Business Profile law firm listing and fill out all the fields." Congratulations, you've done what every other firm on your block also did. That gets you into the game. It doesn't win it.

Local pack placement—the three results that show up in the map section before organic listings—is driven by three things Google has been transparent about: **relevance, distance, and prominence**. You can't move your office. You can influence relevance and prominence significantly. Most firms don't bother with the mechanisms that actually do it.

---

## The Category Problem Nobody Talks About

Your **primary category** is the single most influential field in your entire profile. "Law firm" is a category. So is "Personal injury attorney," "Family law attorney," "Criminal justice attorney," and a dozen others. The firms that rank for specific queries have chosen a specific primary category that matches those queries.

If you're a personal injury firm running a primary category of "Law firm," you are voluntarily handicapping yourself. Pick the category that matches your most valuable case type. Then add secondary categories for everything else you handle.

Change it, watch your rankings move. It's one of the few direct levers you have.

---

## Review Management Is an Active Sport

Reviews are not a set-it-and-forget-it channel. **Volume, recency, and response rate** all factor into how Google weights your profile. A firm with 200 reviews and nothing newer than 18 months is not in the same position as a firm with 80 reviews and 12 posted in the last 90 days.

A few things that matter and most firms skip:

**Responding to every review.** Not with a template. Responding to positive reviews with a name-and-detail acknowledgment signals activity to Google and signals care to prospective clients reading them. Responding to negative reviews professionally—without being defensive or violating confidentiality—demonstrates judgment, which is exactly what someone hiring a lawyer is evaluating.

**Your ask cadence.** If your intake process doesn't include a review request at case resolution, you're leaving your competitors' favor. Build it into the workflow. Text is more effective than email. Timing matters—ask when sentiment is highest, not 60 days after the fact.

**Don't keyword-stuff your responses.** It looks bad to humans, and Google doesn't reward it the way people assume.

## Google Posts: Consistent Signal, Underused by Virtually Every Firm

Google Posts expire after seven days if you use the standard event type, or stay live on your profile as updates. Most firms publish zero posts per month. Some publish a burst when they first set up the profile and then go dark.

This is an opportunity. **Regular posting signals an active, relevant business.** It also gives you a place to surface practice area content, case results (carefully worded to avoid bar compliance issues), community involvement, and answers to common client questions.

What actually works for law firms:

- **"What to do if..." posts** tied to the situations that generate your most common intake calls
- Links back to practice area pages on your website (which also drives clicks that factor into prominence signals)
- Posts timed around seasonal or news-cycle relevance—personal injury firms posting around holiday traffic spikes, for instance

Make sure the pages you're linking to are actually built to convert. If your practice area pages are thin or generic, [practice area landing pages that convert—not just rank—](/blog/law-firm-landing-pages-conversion/) is a direct fix for that problem.

## The Q&A Section Is Either an Asset or a Liability

Here's one most firms have genuinely never thought about: **anyone can post a question on your Google Business Profile, and anyone can answer it.** Including your competitors. Including a disgruntled former client. Including a bot.

The Q&A section surfaces publicly and appears directly on your profile. If you're not monitoring it and seeding it yourself, you're letting strangers write your marketing copy.

What to do:

1. **Seed it yourself.** Post the most common questions you get during intake and answer them thoroughly. "What's the cost for an initial consultation?" "Do you handle cases in [county]?" "How long does a [practice area] case typically take?"
2. **Monitor it weekly.** Google doesn't alert you reliably. Use a tool or build a manual check into your process.
3. **Upvote your own seeded questions** (you can do this) so they appear prominently.

## Multiple Locations and the Practice Area Profile Question

To answer the question directly: **no, you should not create multiple Google Business Profiles for the same office split by practice area.** Google prohibits it. Profiles created this way get suspended, and recovering a suspended profile is a miserable process that can take months.

If you have multiple physical offices, each with staff and client-facing operations, those can have their own profiles. Virtual offices and shared coworking addresses are a different story—Google has tightened its guidelines here, and a profile built on an address where you don't actually have a staffed presence is suspension bait.

If your firm is managing multiple legitimate office locations, NAP consistency—**Name, Address, Phone number**—across your website, GBP profiles, and every directory listing has to be exact. Not approximate. Exact.

## Photos and Video: The Signal Most Firms Treat as Optional

Profile completeness matters. Profiles with photos receive more direction requests and website clicks than those without—Google has published this. The why is simple: photos indicate a real, active business.

For law firms: **office exterior, interior, attorney headshots, and team photos** are the baseline. Short video walkthroughs of the office perform well. Google's algorithm treats frequent photo additions as an activity signal, much like posts.

Do not use stock photography. Google can detect it. More importantly, prospective clients can detect it, and it undermines trust immediately.
