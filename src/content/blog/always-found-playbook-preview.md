## Chapter 2 Preview: The GBP Field Most Firms Get Wrong

The single most important field in your entire Google Business Profile is the primary category. Get this wrong and the rest of the work is wasted — Google won't surface you for the searches you actually want.

A common pattern: a firm with a strong personal injury practice picks "Law Firm" as the primary category because it feels broad and safe. That's the wrong move. "Law Firm" is a low-relevance category that competes with everything from estate planning solos to immigration shops. The firms ranking in the Map Pack for "personal injury lawyer near me" almost always have "Personal Injury Attorney" set as their primary category.

The playbook includes a lookup table for every common practice area and the recommended primary category — Divorce Lawyer for family law, DUI Attorney for DUI-focused firms, Estate Planning Attorney for estate, and so on. It also covers the secondary category strategy, which is where you cover the rest of your practice mix without diluting your primary signal.

This single field decision often moves rankings within 30 days. It's the highest-ROI change in the entire playbook, and it's free.

For context on how this connects to broader GBP optimization, see [local SEO for law firms](/blog/local-seo-law-firms/) — that post lays out the strategic case for why this matters. The playbook is the execution layer underneath it.

## Chapter 3 Preview: Why NAP Consistency Is Less Forgiving Than You Think

Name, Address, Phone — the three fields that have to be identical across every directory listing your firm appears in. Not approximately identical. Identically identical.

The reason this matters: Google cross-references your business across the web. If Avvo shows your address as "123 Main St #200" and Justia shows it as "123 Main Street, Suite 200" and Yelp shows "123 Main Street Ste 200" — those look like three different businesses to a citation aggregator. Your prominence signal gets fragmented across listings that Google can't confidently match to a single entity.

The playbook includes the full 50-source citation list, ranked into three tiers, and the audit process for finding every existing listing — including the duplicates and old-address listings that quietly hurt rankings.

Tier 1 (the first ten you should hit) covers the obvious ones — Google, Bing, Apple Business Connect, Yelp, Facebook — and the legal-specific ones that actually drive client calls: Avvo, FindLaw, Justia, Martindale, BBB. Tier 2 and Tier 3 cover the longer tail of directories that feed data aggregators downstream.

The audit process — five search queries you run in Google to surface every existing mention of your firm, including the old phone numbers and addresses that nobody at the firm remembers are still out there — is the part most firms skip and the part that produces the biggest ranking lift.

This is also the chapter where the four data aggregators (Data Axle, Neustar Localeze, Foursquare, Factual) get explained. Submitting corrections to those four can fix dozens of downstream citations automatically. It's not glamorous work, but it's the foundation everything else rests on. The same logic applies to the [schema markup work](/blog/law-firm-schema-markup/) — the boring technical layer is doing the actual ranking work; the visible stuff sits on top of it.

## Chapter 5 Preview: The Review Velocity Benchmark Nobody Publishes

Most law firms know reviews matter. Almost none of them know what "enough" looks like — and that ambiguity is why the topic sits on a to-do list for years without progress.

The playbook gives you the velocity benchmark: a steady cadence of three to eight new Google reviews per month is what separates firms that hold Map Pack positions from firms that watch rankings slowly erode. Not a one-time push to fifty reviews followed by silence. A steady drip. Google reads velocity and recency as signals of an active business with a current client base.

The chapter walks through:

- The exact email template for the post-case review request (two paragraphs, one link, no fluff)
- The SMS version for clients who don't open email
- The timing rule — when to send the first ask, when to follow up, when to stop
- The vanity URL setup (yourfirm.com/review) that takes a client straight to the Google review form
- The response templates for positive reviews, negative reviews, and the tricky middle ones

The negative review section is where most firms freeze, so the playbook includes a four-part response framework that protects you from bar advertising rule violations while still defending the firm. It also covers when to flag a review for removal and when to leave it alone — most flag attempts fail, and unsuccessful flag attempts can make things worse.
