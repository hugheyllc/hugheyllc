You should switch law firm marketing agencies when you see consistent patterns of non-performance, not just a bad month. The key questions to ask your current agency are: What specific actions did you take last month that drove measurable results? And who owns the assets we've paid for? If you can't get clear answers to both, you likely have a deeper problem. These seven law firm marketing agency problems are the ones I've seen kill partnerships slowly, then all at once.

## 1. Reports Disappear or Become Useless

When an agency stops sending reports, or the reports arrive with vague metrics like "impressions" and "engagement," something is wrong. In accounts I've reviewed, the first sign of trouble is almost always a shift from detailed, actionable reports to one-page summaries that tell you nothing. A good agency sends reports that show cost per lead, conversion trends, and specific actions taken. If you're getting fluff, ask for the raw data. If they can't provide it, that's a law firm marketing agency problem that will only get worse.

## 2. You Own Nothing When You Leave

This is the most expensive mistake I see firms make. Many agencies build websites, landing pages, and ad accounts in their own names. When you leave, you lose everything. I've worked with firms that paid tens of thousands for a site they couldn't take with them. Before you renew anything, check who owns your domain, your Google Ads account, your content, and your email list. If it's not you, that's a hard stop. For more on this, read about [what to look for in marketing contracts before you sign](/blog/law-firm-marketing-contract-red-flags/).

## 3. The Same Strategy for Every Client

A firm I consulted with hired an agency that ran the exact same Facebook ads for a personal injury practice and a corporate defense practice. The agency claimed they had a "proven system." What they really had was a template they refused to customize. If your agency can't explain why your strategy differs from their other clients, you're paying for convenience, not expertise. This is one of those law firm marketing agency problems that signals the agency stopped thinking about your business.

## 4. You Have to Chase Them for Updates

If you're the one initiating every check-in and asking for status updates, the relationship is already broken. A good agency keeps you informed proactively. They flag issues before you notice them. In firms I've consulted with, the ones that waited too long on this problem ended up missing entire quarters of marketing activity because the agency was quietly understaffed or distracted. You shouldn't have to manage your agency. They should manage your account.

## 5. They Blame the Algorithm, the Platform, or the Economy

Every agency hits rough patches. The good ones say, "We tried X, it didn't work, so we pivoted to Y and here are the results." The bad ones blame Google's latest update, Facebook's algorithm change, or the economy. I've seen agencies blame a slow month on the weather. If your agency can't take ownership of outcomes and show you what they're doing differently, you have an accountability problem — and it's often [the reason firms eventually fire their marketing agency](/blog/why-law-firms-fire-marketing-agency/).

## 6. They Resist Audits and Third-Party Reviews

When you ask for an independent audit of your ad accounts or analytics, the agency should welcome it. If they push back, stall, or get defensive, that tells you everything. In accounts I've reviewed, agencies that fight audits are usually hiding poor performance, overbilling, or basic mistakes like broken tracking codes. A transparent agency hands over access and says, "Please check." If yours doesn't, consider it a serious warning. You can start by [running a 30-minute audit of your agency's dashboard](/blog/30-minute-marketing-audit-agency-dashboard/) on your own.

## 7. Your Calls Go to an Account Manager Who Can't Make Decisions

The person you talk to weekly has a title like "Account Manager" or "Client Success Specialist." They're friendly. They take notes. But they can't change strategy, adjust budgets, or approve new creative. All decisions go to someone you never meet. This is a classic law firm marketing agency problem. You're paying for senior-level strategy and getting junior-level handholding. Insist on direct access to the person making decisions about your money. If they refuse, it's time to [negotiate better terms with your agency](/blog/law-firm-marketing-agency-negotiation/) or walk.

## When to Stay, When to Leave

Not every problem means you should fire your agency. A single bad month or a miscommunication can be fixed. But when you see multiple patterns from this list — especially around ownership, accountability, and transparency — the cost of staying is higher than the cost of leaving. You're losing cases, wasting money, and burning time you could spend on clients.

Before you make a move, do one thing. Ask your agency for a complete list of everything they own on your behalf. Domains, ad accounts, analytics, content, email lists. If they hesitate, you have your answer. If they hand it over, run a quick audit. Compare what you're paying to what you're getting. Then decide.
