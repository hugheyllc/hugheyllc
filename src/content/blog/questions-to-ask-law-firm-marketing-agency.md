The most important questions to ask a law firm marketing agency before signing a contract are: Who owns the website and data if we leave? How do you measure success — leads or retained clients? Do you work with competing firms in my market? What's the minimum contract term? And how do you report results? The answers to these questions reveal whether you're hiring a partner invested in your growth or a vendor optimized for their own revenue. Most law firms sign contracts without asking any of them, then spend 12 months discovering the answers the hard way.

I've spent over 20 years watching law firms hire, fire, and re-hire marketing agencies. The firms that get it right almost always ask better questions upfront. The [FTC's guidelines on deceptive advertising practices](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/ftcguide-endorsements-testimonials-advertising) are worth understanding too — because some of what agencies call "results" wouldn’t survive scrutiny under those standards. The firms that get burned almost always skipped the vetting. Here are the 10 questions that separate a good agency relationship from an expensive mistake.

If you're debating whether an [agency or an independent consultant](/blog/agency-vs-consultant-law-firm-marketing/) is the right fit in the first place, start there. But if you've decided to go the agency route, bring this list to every pitch meeting.

## 1. Who Owns the Website If We Part Ways?

**Good answer:** "You own everything — domain, hosting, code, content. We'll hand it all over."

**Bad answer:** "We build on our proprietary platform." Or any hesitation at all.

If you don't own your website, you don't have a marketing asset. You're renting one. And when you leave, you start over from zero. This is the single most common trap in legal marketing, and it costs firms years of [SEO equity they've built up over time](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/fundamentals/how-search-works).

## 2. How Do You Measure Success?

**Good answer:** "We track cost-per-retained-client and tie our reporting to signed cases."

**Bad answer:** "We focus on impressions, traffic, and leads."

Leads are not clients. Traffic is not revenue. If the agency defines success by activity metrics rather than business outcomes, their incentives don't align with yours. Period.

## 3. Do You Work With Other Firms in My Market and Practice Area?

**Good answer:** "No, we have exclusivity policies by market and practice area." Or full transparency about who else they represent.

**Bad answer:** "We can't disclose that." Or: "We have a large portfolio of legal clients."

If an agency is running Google Ads for three personal injury firms in Tampa, they're bidding against themselves with your money. Exclusivity matters. If they won't guarantee it, you need to know that before you sign.

## 4. What's the Minimum Contract Length?

**Good answer:** "Month-to-month after an initial 90-day ramp period."

**Bad answer:** "12 months minimum, auto-renews annually."

Long contracts protect the agency, not you. Some ramp time is reasonable — SEO doesn't produce results in 30 days. But a 12-month lock-in with auto-renewal means they have no incentive to earn your business after the ink dries. Look for contracts that keep the agency accountable every month.

## 5. Do You Mark Up Ad Spend?

**Good answer:** "No. You pay Google directly. Our management fee is separate and transparent."

**Bad answer:** "We handle all billing." Or any structure where you don't see the actual ad spend.

Some agencies take a percentage of your ad spend as their fee. That means they're financially incentivized to increase your budget whether or not it produces results. Others mark up the media spend itself, adding 15-30% you never see. [Google's Partner program](https://support.google.com/google-ads/answer/9128669) requires agencies to disclose their fee structures — ask to see your firm's direct account access in Google Ads, not just a dashboard the agency controls.

## 6. How Will You Report Results?

**Good answer:** "Monthly reports showing spend by channel, leads by source, consultations booked, and clients retained — with source attribution."

**Bad answer:** "We send a monthly report with rankings and traffic data."

If the agency can't [report beyond vanity metrics](/blog/marketing-reports-fake/), they're either not tracking the right data or they don't want you to see the real numbers. You need reporting that connects dollars spent to clients signed. Anything less is a brochure, not a business report.

## 7. What Happens to Our Content If We Leave?

**Good answer:** "All content created for your firm is yours. We'll export everything."

**Bad answer:** "Content created under our retainer is our intellectual property."

Some agencies retain ownership of blog posts, landing pages, and even ad copy they created for you. That means when you leave, your blog disappears, your landing pages go dark, and your SEO craters. Get content ownership in writing before you sign.

## 8. Who Will Actually Be Working on Our Account?

**Good answer:** Specific names, titles, and experience levels. A dedicated point of contact.

**Bad answer:** "Our team." No specifics about who does the work.

The person in the pitch meeting is rarely the person managing your account day-to-day. You might get sold by a senior strategist and managed by a junior coordinator running 30 accounts. Ask who will do the work, how many other accounts they manage, and what their background in legal marketing looks like.

## 9. Can You Show Us Results From a Similar Firm?

**Good answer:** Specific case studies with real numbers — spend, leads, retained clients, timeframe. Ideally from a firm in a similar practice area and market size.

**Bad answer:** "We've worked with hundreds of law firms." Generic testimonials with no data.

Every agency claims to specialize in legal marketing. Few can prove it with numbers. Ask for a case study from a firm comparable to yours — similar size, practice area, and market. If they can only show vanity metrics (traffic increased 200%), push for retention and revenue data.

## 10. What's Your Process for the First 90 Days?

**Good answer:** A clear, phased plan — audit, strategy, implementation milestones, and specific deliverables with dates.

**Bad answer:** "We'll get started right away and you'll see results in a few months."

The first 90 days reveal everything about how an agency operates. A good agency has a structured onboarding process: audit your current performance, identify quick wins, build a strategy, and set measurable goals. A bad agency takes your money and disappears into a black box.

## The Pattern You're Looking For

Read through those answers again. The good answers all have something in common: transparency, accountability, and alignment with your business outcomes.

The bad answers all share a different trait: they protect the agency's revenue at the expense of your visibility into what's actually working.

You're not looking for a vendor who executes tasks. You're looking for a partner whose success depends on your success. The questions above are designed to reveal which one you're talking to.
