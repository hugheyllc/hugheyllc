The question isn't which model is bigger or cheaper—it's which one puts skin in the game when the leads don't show up.

When a law firm marketing agency underperforms, they change the deck. When an independent consultant underperforms, you typically have a more direct conversation about what went wrong. That difference matters more than most firms realize.

The real distinction between a **law firm marketing agency and a consultant** sits in three places: structure, risk, and who actually answers when things stall. Understanding those differences before you hire determines whether you own the relationship or get owned by it.

## What Agencies and Consultants Actually Do Differently

An agency is a business that sells marketing services to multiple clients. A consultant is typically a single operator—or a very small team—who works directly with a handful of firms at once.

That structure creates different incentives.

Agencies have overhead: staff, facilities, account managers, creative directors, the whole org chart. That overhead gets distributed across clients. Consultants have lower overhead but less capacity. An agency can tell you "we need to hire another developer" and bill you for it. A consultant has to choose: do I take on this client, or do I stay with my existing three?

Both models can work. But the accountability is structurally different.

When you hire an agency, you're hiring a business unit. When you hire a consultant, you're often hiring an individual operator who's betting their reputation on your account performing. That matters when results slow down.

## The Accountability Gap

Here's what I see consistently: agencies tend to interpret "underperformance" as a reason to recommend more services. A consultant typically interprets it as a reason to dig into what's broken.

Neither is universal—there are good agencies and bad consultants, and vice versa. But the structural incentive is there.

An agency might say: "Your conversion rate is low. We should redesign your landing pages" (new project). A consultant might say: "Your conversion rate is low. Let me audit your current process and see where people are dropping off" (investigation, then action).

One model scales easily. One model scales slowly but stays connected to results.

When you're evaluating a **law firm marketing agency versus an independent consultant**, ask this: If this relationship fails to generate leads in six months, who has more incentive to tell you the truth about why? The operator who doesn't have a full-time staff to feed, or the one who does?

That's not rhetorical. It matters.

## The Structure That Determines Risk

Agencies typically work on retainers tied to service delivery, not results. You pay for hours, strategy sessions, and monthly reports. The agency delivers the service—the results are your responsibility, or at least shared in a way that's hard to measure.

[Many firms end up with underperforming retainers because the metrics used to measure success don't connect to actual business outcomes](/blog/law-firm-marketing-retainer-underdelivery/). The agency shows traffic up. Leads might be flat. The retainer keeps rolling.

A consultant usually works one of two ways: retainer tied to ongoing execution, or project-based work tied to a deliverable. Either way, if you're not seeing progress, the conversation is immediate and personal. There's no account management layer between you and the person making decisions.

That directness is the real trade-off. You get faster feedback but less scale. You don't get a team of specialists across disciplines—you get one person who has to be competent across many.

## When You Actually Need an Agency

An agency makes sense if:

- You need multiple disciplines executed in parallel (PPC, SEO, content, design, reputation management, paid social) and you want one vendor managing the integration.
- Your firm is large enough that you need dedicated resources assigned to your account.
- You want the agency to absorb the hiring risk—they scale their team, you don't.

The risk: you're paying for coordination overhead, and that overhead doesn't always translate to better results. It just translates to a bigger invoice.

## When a Consultant Makes More Sense

A consultant typically makes sense if:

- You already know what the core problems are (intake speed, landing page conversion, lead quality) and you want someone to execute the fix.
- You want direct access to the person making decisions.
- You're willing to trade some service-delivery breadth for tighter accountability on the areas that move the needle.
- You want someone who'll tell you the hard truth: "This channel isn't working for your firm, and we should stop spending here."

The risk: you're betting on one person's competence across multiple areas. If they're weak in a critical area, you have to hire another vendor anyway.

## Asking the Right Questions Before You Hire

Before you sign with either model, ask these:

**1. How do you define success for my firm?**
The honest answer should reference leads, conversions, or revenue. Not traffic, impressions, or "awareness." If they lead with vanity metrics, they're selling activity, not results.

**2. When results don't materialize, what's the first step?**
Good agencies and consultants audit before they recommend changes. Bad ones recommend more work. Listen to what they describe.

**3. Who am I talking to every month?**
If an agency says "your account manager will update you," ask if you can speak to the person actually doing the work. If they say no, that's a red flag. If a consultant says "I'll update you quarterly," that's another one.

**4. What does your contract say about underperformance?**
This is critical. [Before you sign any marketing contract, understand what happens if the other side doesn't deliver](/blog/law-firm-marketing-contract-red-flags/). Can you pause billing? Can you negotiate a performance adjustment? Or are you locked in regardless?

**5. How many law firms like mine do you work with?**
You want someone who has pattern recognition in your practice area. An agency might say "we work with 30 law firms." A consultant might say "I work with two other family law firms, both in similar-size markets." The second answer is more useful—they know what works in your specific scenario.

## The Middle Ground: Hybrid Relationships

Some firms hire an agency for scale on one channel (Google Ads, for instance) and a consultant for strategy and diagnostics. That can work if the two providers actually talk to each other. Often they don't, and you end up with disconnected execution.

If you go hybrid, make the two providers talk monthly. Make sure they share the same definition of a "good lead." Otherwise you're just paying twice.

## The Real Accountability Question

At the end of the line, the question is: When something doesn't work, who has the most incentive to figure out why and fix it?

An agency is incentivized to keep you on retainer and recommend new services.

A consultant is incentivized to make you successful or lose the relationship.

Both incentives can produce good work. But they're not the same. Know which one you're betting on before you sign.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Should I hire an agency or a consultant for my law firm?
It depends on your current needs and tolerance for hands-on oversight. If you need multiple marketing disciplines executed together and want a dedicated team, an agency makes sense. If you know your core problems and want direct access to decision-makers, a consultant is usually faster and more transparent. The real question is accountability—who's incentivized to tell you the truth when something isn't working.

### What's the typical cost difference?
Agencies typically charge $3,000–$15,000+ per month depending on scope and firm size. Consultants often charge $2,000–$8,000 per month for ongoing work, or $5,000–$25,000 for discrete projects. Cost isn't the deciding factor—the relationship structure and accountability matter more than the price.

### Can I start with a consultant and move to an agency later?
Yes, and it's often smart. Consultants can help you audit what's working and what's not, then you can brief an agency on those findings if you want to scale. This approach also lets you validate assumptions before you commit to a larger retainer.

### What should I ask about in the first conversation?
Ask how they define success (leads or revenue, not traffic), what they do when results stall, who you'll be talking to monthly, what underperformance clauses are in the contract, and how many firms like yours they currently work with. These answers tell you more than their pitch ever will.

### How do I know if my current agency is underperforming?
[Run a 30-minute audit of your agency's dashboard to see what metrics they're actually moving](/blog/30-minute-marketing-audit-agency-dashboard/). Compare the leads they're claiming to generate against the leads actually hitting your intake process. If those numbers don't match, the agency is either misreporting or the handoff is broken. Either way, it's a problem you need to address.
