# Mobile-First Indexing and Law Firm Websites: Is Yours Actually Mobile-Friendly?

**Law firm mobile SEO and mobile-first indexing** aren't optional considerations anymore—they're the baseline. Here's the direct answer: Google uses the mobile version of your website to determine how you rank. Not the desktop version you spent $15,000 designing. The mobile version. If your mobile experience is slow, cluttered, or technically broken, your rankings reflect that whether you know it or not. And the second question managing partners always ask: *Is my law firm website actually mobile-friendly for Google?* The honest answer, for the majority of law firm sites, is no—not fully. It looks fine on a phone. That's not the same thing.

This post covers what **mobile-first indexing** really means in practice, the specific technical failures that hurt law firm websites, and what you actually need to fix.

---

## What Mobile-First Indexing Actually Means

Google completed its rollout of mobile-first indexing some time ago. Every new site indexed by Google is evaluated primarily through its mobile crawl. That means the content Google sees, the links Google counts, and the performance signals Google measures are all pulled from your mobile experience.

The implication most law firms miss: if your mobile site has less content than your desktop site—shorter paragraphs, hidden sections, collapsed practice area descriptions—Google is ranking a stripped-down version of your website. You're not just delivering a worse experience to mobile visitors. You're actively undermining your own SEO.

This is one of the more common reasons law firm sites plateau or drop without any obvious explanation. ## The 'Responsive' Design Problem Nobody Talks About

Every web developer will tell you your site is responsive. What they mean is: the layout shifts for smaller screens. What they don't mean—and what matters for **law firm mobile SEO**—is that the user experience is optimized for how people actually use phones to find legal help.

Those are very different things.

Here's what responsive-but-broken looks like in practice:

**Text that technically fits but is too small to read.** Google's guidelines call for a minimum 16px font size for body text. A surprising number of law firm sites render body copy at 13–14px on mobile. Technically displays. Technically unreadable.

**Touch targets that are too close together.** Buttons, phone numbers, nav links—when these are stacked tightly on mobile, users misclick. Google's mobile usability standards flag touch elements that are smaller than 48x48 pixels or spaced fewer than 8 pixels apart. Your mobile visitor trying to tap your phone number and accidentally hitting your blog archive is not converting.

**Content hidden behind tabs or accordions on mobile.** Developers do this to clean up the mobile layout. Google may or may not crawl that hidden content with equal weight. It's a gamble you shouldn't take with your practice area pages.

**Pop-ups and interstitials that cover the screen.** Google has explicitly penalized intrusive interstitials on mobile since 2017. If your site fires a full-screen chat widget or newsletter pop-up immediately on mobile, you have a ranking problem and a user experience problem.

## Core Web Vitals: The Metrics That Are Actually Hurting You

Core Web Vitals are Google's performance benchmarks. Three of them matter most:

**Largest Contentful Paint (LCP)** — How long it takes for the main content on the page to load. For law firm websites, the hero image is almost always the culprit. A 4MB attorney photo that looks sharp on desktop becomes a four-second load time on a mobile connection. Google wants LCP under 2.5 seconds. Most law firm sites on mobile are running 4–7 seconds.

**Interaction to Next Paint (INP)** — How quickly your page responds when someone taps or clicks. Heavy JavaScript, bloated plugins, and third-party chat widgets all drag this number up. A site that *looks* fast but lags when you try to tap a button is failing this metric.

**Cumulative Layout Shift (CLS)** — Whether the page jumps around while loading. You've experienced this: you're about to tap a phone number and the page shifts and you tap an ad instead. Law firm sites frequently fail this because fonts and images load asynchronously and push content down.

You can run your site through Google's PageSpeed Insights or Search Console's Core Web Vitals report to see where you stand. If you're failing on mobile and passing on desktop, that's exactly the split that damages your rankings under mobile-first indexing.

## What Mobile Visitors to Law Firm Websites Actually Need

People searching for a lawyer on their phone are often in a stressful situation. They're not browsing. They want to confirm you handle their type of case, verify you're local, and call or contact you within 60 seconds of landing on your page.

Your mobile site needs to make that path frictionless. That means:

- **A tap-to-call phone number visible above the fold, always.** Not buried in a hamburger menu.
- **Clear practice area identification within the first screen.** If someone lands on your personal injury page, the word "personal injury" and your location should be visible without scrolling.
- **Fast-loading pages.** Not fast for a law firm. Fast, period.
- **Forms that actually work on mobile.** Multi-column form layouts break on small screens. Autofill should work. The submit button should be reachable without zooming.

## How to Actually Test This

Stop asking your web developer if your site is mobile-friendly. Run the tests yourself:

1. **Google Search Console → Mobile Usability report.** Shows actual crawl errors on your live site.
2. **PageSpeed Insights (pagespeed.web.dev).** Run your homepage, your top practice area page, and your contact page. Look at the Mobile tab specifically.
3. **Google's Mobile-Friendly Test tool.** Basic but useful for a quick viewport and rendering check.
4. **Your own phone.** Open the site in Chrome on your actual phone. Throttle the connection to "Fast 3G" in developer tools if you want to simulate a realistic mobile experience. Be honest about what you see.

If the results are bad, you're not alone—but you do need to fix it. This isn't a cosmetic issue. It's a ranking issue with a direct line to whether your phone rings.
