## How Many Fields, and Why Prospects Aren't Filling Them Out

A law firm contact form should have **three to five fields** in most cases. Name, phone number or email, and a brief description of the matter. That's it. You don't need their date of birth, how they heard about you, or their preferred callback window before you've even spoken to them. **Law firm contact form conversion** rates drop measurably with every field you add past five — not because people are lazy, but because every extra field signals effort, and effort signals risk. Prospects in legal distress are already anxious. Your form is the first test of whether working with your firm is going to be painful or easy.

Why are prospects not filling out your contact form? Usually one of three reasons: the form asks for too much too soon, it doesn't feel secure, or it's buried three clicks deep on a page that wasn't designed to convert. None of those are traffic problems. They're friction problems — and friction is fixable.

## The Friction Audit: What's Actually Killing Your Conversions

Before you redesign anything, you need to understand where people are dropping off. Most law firms don't have this data. They look at overall website traffic, feel good about the numbers, and assume leads are flowing. They're not connecting the dots between visits and submissions.

Here's what to look for:

- **Field count.** Count every required field. If you're above five for an initial inquiry form, you're over the line.
- **Mobile rendering.** Pull up your form on a real phone. Does the keyboard obscure fields? Do dropdowns work? Is the submit button visible without scrolling? If you haven't checked recently, do it now. [Mobile-first indexing affects more than just rankings](/blog/law-firm-mobile-first-indexing-seo/) — a form that breaks on mobile is a form that doesn't convert.
- **Page load speed.** A form on a slow page doesn't get seen. If your contact page takes more than three seconds to load, a meaningful percentage of prospects are already gone.
- **Placement.** If your contact form only exists on a dedicated Contact page, you're leaving conversions on the table. Forms belong on practice area pages too — close to where the decision happens.
- **Trust signals.** Is there anything near the form that reduces anxiety? Attorney credentials, a brief privacy note, a simple reassurance about confidentiality? Absence of trust signals is its own kind of friction.

## What Questions Should Actually Be on a Law Firm Intake Form

The goal of an initial contact form is not to gather all the information you'll eventually need. It's to lower the barrier to the first conversation.

For most practice areas, a form that converts well asks:

1. **Full name**
2. **Best way to reach you** (phone or email — let them choose)
3. **Brief description of your situation** (a single open text field, not a laundry list of dropdowns)

Optionally: **Preferred contact time**, if your intake team genuinely honors it. Don't ask for it if you're calling whenever it's convenient for you.

Noticeably absent: matter type dropdown menus with seventeen options, referral source questions, case value estimates, jurisdiction fields. All of that can be collected during intake. Your form's only job is to get someone to raise their hand.

If you want to qualify leads before they hit your calendar, add one conditional question — something like "Are you the person affected, or are you contacting us on someone's behalf?" That's genuinely useful and adds minimal friction.

## The Copy Around the Form Matters as Much as the Form Itself

This is the part most firms ignore entirely. The headline above your form, the subhead, and the button text are doing real conversion work — or they're not doing any.

**Avoid:** "Contact Us" as a button label. It's the weakest possible instruction.

**Use:** Something that signals next step and reduces perceived commitment. "Get a Free Consultation," "Tell Us What Happened," "Schedule Your Call." These aren't just word choices — they change the psychological framing of the action.

The paragraph above the form should answer an unspoken question the prospect has: *What happens after I submit this?* Tell them. "One of our attorneys will review your submission and reach out within one business day." Specific, credible, low-pressure.

## Response Time Is a Conversion Variable Too

Your form conversion rate doesn't end at submission. It ends when a prospect becomes a client. If someone fills out your form and doesn't hear back for 48 hours, the conversion is lost — even though the form did its job.

This is an operational problem that masquerades as a marketing problem. Firms blame their website for low lead quality when the real issue is that their intake process lets warm leads go cold.

If you're running paid search traffic to your contact pages — and you should be comparing [the math between paid search and organic SEO for law firms](/blog/paid-search-vs-seo-law-firms/) before you scale spend — a slow response time is burning real money on every delayed follow-up.

Setting expectations on the form itself helps. A simple line like "We respond to all inquiries within one business day" manages anxiety and reduces the likelihood that a prospect fires off three more contact forms to your competitors while waiting.

## A/B Testing: The Part Firms Skip

Most law firm websites have never run a single A/B test on their contact forms. They launch a form during a redesign — and then leave it untouched for three years.

You don't need sophisticated tooling to start. Test one variable at a time:

- Button text ("Get a Free Consultation" vs. "Talk to an Attorney")
- Number of fields (three vs. five)
- Form placement on the page (above the fold vs. after substantive content)
- Headline above the form

Track submissions, not just visits. If you're not measuring **law firm contact form conversion** rates at the page level, you're flying blind. This connects directly to the broader problem of [tracking marketing ROI with metrics that actually matter](/blog/law-firm-marketing-roi-metrics/) — form conversion rate is a metric worth monitoring weekly, not quarterly.

## The Technical Layer You Probably Haven't Checked

Forms break. Not dramatically — they just quietly stop submitting, or stop sending notifications, or start routing to a spam folder. This happens more often than firms realize, especially after website updates.

Check:

- **Confirmation messages.** Does the page display a thank-you message or redirect after submission? If not, users don't know the form worked.
- **Email notifications.** Are submissions actually arriving in the right inbox? Test it monthly.
- **Spam filtering.** Are legitimate submissions getting flagged? Review your spam folder.
- **Form errors.** Do field validation messages appear clearly when someone makes an input error, or does the form just silently refuse to submit?

These are unglamorous checks. They're also the kind of [technical issue that quietly kills performance](/blog/technical-seo-law-firms-overlooked/) while everyone's attention is on content and ads.
