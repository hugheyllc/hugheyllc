Chapter 1: Why Reviews Are Your Most Powerful Local Ranking Signal

How Google Uses Reviews in Map Pack Rankings

When someone searches "personal injury lawyer near me" or "divorce attorney Tampa," Google returns a Map Pack — the three local business listings that appear above organic results. The Map Pack captures the majority of clicks for local legal searches. Getting into it is the single highest-ROI local marketing activity a law firm can pursue.

Google's local ranking algorithm weighs three primary factors: relevance, distance, and prominence. Reviews are the dominant signal within prominence, and they influence it across five dimensions:

  1. Quantity — More reviews signal a more established, trusted business. A firm with 150 reviews outranks a firm with 15, all else being equal.
  2. Rating — Average star rating matters, but less than most firms think. A 4.7 with 200 reviews consistently outperforms a 5.0 with 12 reviews.
  3. Recency — Google weights recent reviews more heavily than old ones. A firm that received 10 reviews this month ranks better than one that received 50 reviews two years ago and none since.
  4. Velocity — The rate at which new reviews arrive. Steady, consistent new reviews signal an active, thriving business.
  5. Response rate — Whether and how quickly the business responds to reviews. Google tracks this and uses it as a quality signal.

Of these five, recency and velocity are the most underappreciated by law firms. Most firms focus on total count and star rating. The firms dominating the Map Pack focus on getting new reviews every single week.

The Compounding Effect

Review velocity compounds. Here's why:

A firm that gets 4 reviews per month for 24 months ends up with 96 reviews. But the ranking benefit isn't just about reaching 96. Google sees a business that has been consistently active, consistently receiving client feedback, and consistently engaging with that feedback for two full years. That behavioral pattern is itself a ranking signal.

Meanwhile, a competing firm might run a one-time review campaign, jump from 20 to 60 reviews in a month, then stop. Three months later, Google sees a business with stale reviews and no recent activity. The velocity firm overtakes them despite having fewer total reviews, because its signal is fresh and consistent.

A common pattern in accounts I've reviewed: firms with lower total review counts but steady monthly velocity occupy Map Pack positions above firms with higher totals but dormant profiles. Consistency beats campaigns every time.

Reviews as Trust Signals for Prospective Clients

Beyond rankings, reviews drive conversion. When a potential client lands on your Google Business Profile, they're making a decision. Here's what they actually pay attention to:

What prospective clients read:

  • The most recent 3–5 reviews (not your oldest glowing review from 2019)
  • Any review that mentions a specific practice area matching their need
  • Negative reviews and how the firm responded
  • Whether the firm actually replies to reviews at all

What prospective clients skip:

  • Reviews older than 6 months (they scan past them)
  • Generic "great lawyer" reviews with no detail
  • Reviews that look templated or fake (all posted on the same day, similar language)

The implication is clear: you need a steady stream of recent, detailed, authentic reviews — and you need to respond to every single one.

The Review Gap

Despite reviews being this important, most law firms are dramatically under-reviewed. A common pattern across the legal industry: firms that have practiced for 10 or 15 years have fewer than 30 Google reviews. Some have fewer than 10.

This happens for predictable reasons:

  • No system. The firm relies on clients voluntarily leaving reviews. Without a prompt, the review rate is close to zero.
  • Discomfort. Attorneys feel awkward asking for reviews, especially in sensitive practice areas like family law or criminal defense.
  • Timing. By the time a case closes, the firm has moved on mentally, and nobody follows up.
  • Turnover. The person who used to handle marketing left, and the process left with them.
  • Fear of negative reviews. Firms avoid asking because they're afraid of the occasional unhappy client. (Chapter 4 addresses why this fear is misplaced.)

The review gap is your opportunity. In most legal markets, a firm that implements even a basic review request system will surpass the majority of competitors within 6–12 months.

AI Search and Reviews

This section matters more in 2026 than it would have two years ago. AI-powered search tools — ChatGPT with browsing, Perplexity, Google's AI Overviews — are increasingly how people find and evaluate service providers.

When someone asks an AI tool "who's the best personal injury lawyer in Tampa," the AI pulls from multiple signals, but review data is heavily weighted because it's structured, publicly available, and quantifiable. AI tools reference:

  • Total review count and average rating from Google
  • Review text content (AI can read and summarize hundreds of reviews)
  • Recency of reviews
  • Whether the business responds to reviews
  • Review content across multiple platforms

Firms with robust, recent, well-responded-to review profiles are more likely to be surfaced in AI-generated recommendations. This is a new ranking surface that didn't exist three years ago, and it favors the same behaviors that drive Google Map Pack success.

The playbook you're holding works for both traditional search and AI search. The fundamentals are the same: get consistent, authentic reviews, respond to all of them, and maintain velocity.

Chapter 2: Setting Up Your Review Infrastructure

Before you ask a single client for a review, your infrastructure needs to be right. This chapter walks through every platform and tool you need, in order of priority.

Google Business Profile: Claiming, Verifying, and Optimizing

Your Google Business Profile (GBP) is the foundation. If you don't have one claimed and verified, nothing else in this playbook matters.

Step 1: Claim your profile

  1. Go to business.google.com
  2. Search for your firm's name
  3. If a listing exists, click "Claim this business" and follow the verification steps
  4. If no listing exists, click "Add your business" and create one

Step 2: Verify your profile

Google will verify you own the business. Methods include:

  • Postcard by mail — Google mails a postcard with a verification code to your office address. Takes 5–14 days.
  • Phone verification — Available for some businesses. Google calls or texts a code.
  • Video verification — Google may ask you to record a short video of your office and signage.

Do not skip verification. An unverified profile cannot collect reviews or appear in Map Pack results.

Step 3: Optimize your profile

Once verified, complete every field:

Field What to Enter
Business name Your firm's legal name exactly as it appears on signage. Do not stuff keywords.
Category Primary: your main practice area (e.g., "Personal injury attorney"). Add up to 9 secondary categories.
Address Your physical office address. If you serve clients at their location, set a service area instead.
Phone Your main office number. Use a local number, not a toll-free number.
Website Your firm's homepage URL.
Hours Accurate office hours. Update for holidays.
Description 750 characters. Lead with your practice areas and location. No keyword stuffing.
Photos Exterior, interior, team photos. Minimum 10 photos. Update quarterly.

How to Create a Direct Review Link

A direct review link opens Google Maps with the review dialog already active. This removes friction — the client doesn't have to search for your firm or figure out where to click.

Step-by-step:

  1. Go to Google Maps (maps.google.com)
  2. Search for your firm's name
  3. Click on your firm's listing
  4. Click "Reviews" to see your existing reviews
  5. Look at the URL in your browser. Find the string after place/ — this is your Place ID reference
  6. Your direct review link format is:
https://search.google.com/local/writereview?placeid=YOUR_PLACE_ID

To find your exact Place ID:

  1. Go to: https://developers.google.com/maps/documentation/places/web-service/place-id
  2. Use the Place ID Finder tool
  3. Search for your firm
  4. Copy the Place ID (it starts with "ChIJ...")
  5. Insert it into the URL template above

Test the link. Open it in an incognito browser window. It should take you directly to a review dialog for your firm. If it doesn't, the Place ID is wrong.

Save this link. You'll use it in every email template, SMS, and QR code in this playbook.

Creating a QR Code for In-Office Use

A QR code that links to your direct review URL lets clients leave a review while they're still in your office — the highest-conversion moment.

  1. Go to a free QR code generator (qr-code-generator.com or similar)
  2. Paste your direct review link
  3. Download the QR code as a high-resolution PNG
  4. Print it on:
    • A small table card for reception and conference rooms
    • The back of business cards
    • A framed sign near the exit
    • Intake packet folders

Design the card simply:

We'd love your feedback.
Scan to leave a Google review.

[QR CODE]

Thank you for choosing [Firm Name].

No incentive language. No "get a discount." Just a clean ask. (Chapter 4 explains why.)

Setting Up Notifications

You need to know about every new review within hours, not days.

Google Business Profile notifications:

  1. Open the Google Business Profile Manager (business.google.com)
  2. Go to Settings → Notifications
  3. Enable "Customer reviews" notifications
  4. Ensure the notification email goes to someone who checks email daily

Google Alerts:

  1. Go to google.com/alerts
  2. Create an alert for your firm's name (in quotes): "Smith & Associates Law Firm"
  3. Set delivery to "As-it-happens"
  4. This catches mentions on third-party sites, not just Google reviews

Assign a specific person to monitor these notifications and respond within 24 hours.

Chapter 3: The Review Request System

This is the core of the playbook. Everything else supports this chapter. A review request system is a repeatable process that ensures every satisfied client is asked to leave a review at the right time, by the right person, through the right channel.

When to Ask: Timing Relative to Case Milestones

Timing is the single biggest factor in whether a client leaves a review. Ask at the wrong time, and you get silence. Ask at the right time, and conversion rates jump dramatically.

The best moments to ask:

Milestone Why It Works How to Ask
Case settled/won Client is at peak satisfaction and gratitude Attorney or lead paralegal delivers the news, then the review ask follows within 48 hours via email
Charges dropped/reduced Relief is a powerful motivator Same as above
Closing/signing completed Client feels a sense of completion In-person handoff of QR code card + follow-up email
Positive check-in call Client expressed satisfaction unprompted "I'm glad to hear that — would you mind sharing that experience on Google?"
Final invoice paid Client has closed the loop financially Follow-up email within 3 days

When NOT to ask:

  • During active litigation or negotiation (client is stressed)
  • Immediately after delivering bad news
  • When a billing dispute is unresolved
  • During the first meeting or consultation (too early)

Who Should Ask

The person who asks matters. The closer the relationship, the higher the response rate.

Ideal hierarchy:

  1. The lead attorney on the case — highest conversion rate, but hardest to systematize
  2. The paralegal or legal assistant who worked directly with the client — strong relationship, easier to build into workflow
  3. Office manager or marketing coordinator — lowest conversion rate for the initial ask, but ideal for follow-ups and system management

The practical approach: The attorney or paralegal mentions the review in conversation ("We'd really appreciate a Google review — it helps other people in your situation find us"). Then the office manager sends the formal email with the link within 48 hours.

This two-touch method — verbal mention followed by email with link — consistently outperforms a cold email alone.

The 3-Request Sequence

Most clients need more than one prompt. Not because they don't want to leave a review, but because they're busy and forget. A three-touch sequence over 14 days captures the majority of willing reviewers without being pushy.

Request 1: Email (Within 1 Week of Positive Milestone)

Send this email within 2–7 days of the case milestone. Don't wait longer — the emotional peak fades.

Subject: A quick favor, [First Name]?

Hi [First Name],

It was a pleasure working with you on your [general case description — e.g., "family matter" or "case"]. I'm glad we could help you reach a positive outcome.

If you have a moment, we'd be grateful if you'd share your experience on Google. It takes about 60 seconds, and it helps other people in similar situations find the help they need.

[Leave a Review →] (link to your direct review URL)

Thank you for trusting [Firm Name]. We wish you all the best.

Warm regards, [Attorney or Staff Name] [Firm Name]

Key elements:

  • Short subject line — conversational, not corporate
  • References the case vaguely (no details that could compromise confidentiality)
  • Frames the review as helping others, not helping the firm
  • One clear call to action
  • Direct link — no extra clicks

Request 2: SMS (7 Days After Email, If No Review)

If the client hasn't left a review after the email, send a brief text message. SMS has a much higher open rate than email.

Hi [First Name], this is [Staff Name] from [Firm Name]. If you have 60 seconds, we'd really appreciate a Google review — it helps others find us. Here's the link: [direct review URL]. Thank you!

Rules for SMS:

  • Only send if the client has consented to text communication (check your intake forms)
  • Send during business hours only
  • One text. Do not follow up via text again.
  • Keep it under 160 characters if possible (link will make it longer, which is fine)

Request 3: Personal Follow-Up (10–14 Days, High-Value Clients Only)

For clients with whom the firm had a strong personal relationship — long-duration cases, significant outcomes, clients who expressed deep gratitude — a personal follow-up from the attorney is appropriate.

This is a phone call or personal email, not a template blast.

Phone script:

"Hi [First Name], it's [Attorney Name]. I wanted to check in and see how things are going since we wrapped up your case. [Brief personal exchange.] I also wanted to mention — if you've had a chance, we'd love a Google review. It really does make a difference for us. I can have [Staff Name] send you the link again if that's easier."

This third touch is optional and should be reserved for clients where the relationship warrants it. Do not use it on every client — it becomes intrusive.

Phone and In-Person Ask Scripts

Sometimes the best ask happens face-to-face or on the phone. Here are scripts for common scenarios:

Attorney closing a case in person:

"[First Name], I'm really glad we could get this resolved for you. If you don't mind, one thing that really helps our firm is Google reviews — when people are looking for a [practice area] attorney, those reviews make a big difference. Would you be open to leaving us one? I'll have [Staff Name] send you the link so it's easy."

Paralegal on a check-in call:

"I'm glad everything is going well. By the way, if you have a spare minute, we'd love it if you could leave us a review on Google. I can text you the link right now if you'd like."

Receptionist at checkout (for transactional practices like estate planning or real estate closings):

"Thank you for coming in today. Before you go — we have a QR code here if you'd like to leave us a Google review. It only takes about a minute, and it really helps us out."

QR Code Handoff Script

When handing a client a QR code card:

"Here's a card with a QR code — if you scan it with your phone camera, it'll take you right to our Google review page. It takes less than a minute, and it makes a huge difference for us. No pressure at all, but we'd appreciate it."

Keep it casual. Smile. Don't hover while they do it.

Batch Request Approach for Older Clients

If you're implementing this system for the first time, you likely have years of past clients who were never asked for a review. You can (and should) reach out to them — but do it carefully.

Rules for batch requests:

  1. Only contact clients whose cases ended positively. Review your files or CRM.
  2. Spread the requests over 4–6 weeks. Sending 200 emails on one day looks suspicious to Google and may trigger review filtering.
  3. Send no more than 15–20 requests per week during the catch-up phase.
  4. Use a slightly different template that acknowledges the time gap:

Subject: Hope you're doing well, [First Name]

Hi [First Name],

It's been a while since we worked together on your [general case description], and I hope things have been going well for you since then.

We're making an effort to hear from past clients, and I wanted to ask — if you had a positive experience with our firm, would you consider leaving us a brief Google review? It takes about 60 seconds.

[Leave a Review →] (link to your direct review URL)

Thank you, and don't hesitate to reach out if we can ever help you again.

Best, [Attorney or Staff Name] [Firm Name]

What Never to Say

Certain language in review requests can trigger Google policy violations, result in reviews being filtered, or even get your profile penalized.

Never say or write:

Prohibited Language Why
"Leave us a 5-star review" Directing the star rating violates Google's policy
"We'll give you a discount/gift card if you review us" Incentivized reviews are prohibited
"Only leave a review if you had a positive experience" This is review gating (see Chapter 4)
"Here's a script/example of what to write" Coaching review content violates policy
"Post this review for us" Writing reviews on behalf of clients is fraud
"We need your help beating [competitor]" Inappropriate and unprofessional
"Please mention [keyword] in your review" Keyword stuffing requests look manipulative

Always safe to say:

  • "We'd appreciate your honest feedback"
  • "If you have a moment, we'd love a Google review"
  • "Your experience helps other people find the help they need"
  • "It takes about 60 seconds"

Chapter 4: Review Gating — What It Is and Why It's Dangerous

Definition

Review gating is the practice of pre-screening clients before directing them to leave a public review. Typically, it works like this: the firm sends a survey asking "How was your experience?" If the client responds positively, they receive a link to Google. If they respond negatively, they're directed to an internal feedback form instead.

This is a violation of Google's policies. Period.

Why Google Prohibits It

Google's review policy explicitly states that businesses cannot "discourage or prohibit negative reviews, or selectively solicit positive reviews from customers." Review gating does exactly that — it filters the review population to only include happy clients, which makes the review profile misleading.

Google detects gating through patterns: when a firm's reviews are almost exclusively 5-star, arrive in bursts after internal surveys, and the firm has no negative reviews despite high volume, it flags the profile for scrutiny. The consequences range from individual reviews being silently removed to the profile being suspended.

Why Bar Associations Care

Beyond Google's rules, review gating raises ethical concerns for attorneys. The American Bar Association's Model Rules of Professional Conduct require that attorney marketing be truthful and not misleading. A review profile that has been artificially filtered to exclude negative feedback could be considered misleading advertising.

Several state bars have issued opinions on attorney review practices. The Florida Bar, for example, requires that testimonials in attorney advertising be truthful and not misleading. A review collection process that systematically excludes dissatisfied clients arguably produces a misleading testimonial profile.

The bottom line: Review gating creates legal and ethical risk for an uncertain benefit. Don't do it.

Common Violations Firms Don't Realize They're Making

Many firms are review gating without knowing it. Watch for these patterns:

  1. The satisfaction survey redirect. Your marketing tool sends a "How did we do?" email. Happy responses get a Google link. Unhappy responses get an internal form. This is textbook gating.

  2. The NPS pre-screen. You send a Net Promoter Score survey. Only promoters (9–10 scores) receive a review request. This is gating.

  3. The verbal pre-screen. Staff are trained to ask "Did you have a good experience?" and only give the QR code to people who say yes. This is gating.

  4. The software default. Some reputation management platforms enable gating by default. If your tool has a "satisfaction threshold" or "minimum rating to request review" setting, it's gating. Turn it off.

The Compliant Alternative

You can absolutely manage the client experience without gating. Here's how:

  1. Ask everyone. Every client with a closed case gets the same review request through the same process. No filtering.

  2. Handle complaints separately. If a client expresses dissatisfaction before you send the review request, address their complaint directly through your normal client service process. Don't withhold the review request — but do try to resolve the issue first as good client service, not as review manipulation.

  3. Respond to every review. When a negative review does appear (and eventually one will), respond professionally using the templates in Chapter 5. A well-handled negative review actually increases trust.

  4. Trust the math. If you're providing good service, your natural ratio will be overwhelmingly positive. A few negative reviews among dozens of positive ones actually makes your profile look more authentic. Clients are suspicious of profiles with only 5-star reviews.

Florida Bar and ABA Rules on Client Testimonials

Key rules that affect review practices for attorneys:

ABA Model Rule 7.1: A lawyer shall not make a false or misleading communication about the lawyer or the lawyer's services.

ABA Model Rule 7.2: A lawyer may pay the reasonable cost of advertising. This does not extend to paying for reviews.

Florida Bar Rule 4-7.13: Testimonials in lawyer advertising must be truthful. If a testimonial implies a result, the ad must include a disclaimer that results vary.

Practical implications for reviews:

  • You cannot pay clients for reviews (gift cards, discounts, fee reductions)
  • You cannot fabricate reviews or have staff write them
  • You cannot ask clients to misrepresent their experience
  • You can ask every client for an honest review
  • You can share reviews on your website (with appropriate disclaimers for result-specific testimonials)
  • You can respond to reviews publicly

When in doubt, the rule is simple: ask everyone, offer nothing in return, and don't coach the content.

Chapter 5: Responding to Reviews

Why Response Rate and Quality Are Ranking Signals

Google has confirmed that responding to reviews improves local ranking. The mechanism is straightforward: response activity signals that the business is engaged, attentive, and actively managed. Google rewards active profiles.

Beyond the ranking benefit, response quality affects conversion. Prospective clients read your responses. A thoughtful response to a positive review reinforces your professionalism. A composed, helpful response to a negative review demonstrates character and maturity. No response at all — to either type — suggests the firm doesn't care.

Industry research consistently shows that businesses that respond to reviews earn more trust from prospective customers than businesses that don't, even when the non-responding business has a higher average rating.

The 24-Hour Response Rule

Respond to every review within 24 business hours. Not 24 hours from when you notice it — 24 hours from when it's posted. This is why the notification system from Chapter 2 matters.

Assign a specific person to review monitoring and response. Give them authority to respond to positive reviews immediately using the templates below. For negative reviews, they should draft a response and get approval from a supervising attorney before posting — but still within 24 hours.

Responding to Positive Reviews

The formula: Acknowledge + Personalize + Reinforce + CTA

  1. Acknowledge — Thank them by name
  2. Personalize — Reference something specific (without revealing case details)
  3. Reinforce — Affirm your firm's values or commitment
  4. CTA — Subtle call to action (referral, return if needed)

⚠️ Critical rule: Never reveal case details, practice area, or any confidential information in a response. Even if the client mentioned their case type in their review, your response should not confirm or expand on it.

Five Example Positive Review Responses

1. General positive review:

Thank you, [Name] — we appreciate you taking the time to share your experience. Our team works hard to make the process as smooth as possible, and it means a lot to hear that it came through. Don't hesitate to reach out if you ever need anything in the future.

2. Client who won their case:

[Name], thank you so much for this kind review. We're glad we could help you through a challenging time, and we're thrilled with the outcome. If you know anyone who could use a hand, we're always here. Wishing you the best.

3. Long or complex process:

Thank you for your patience and trust throughout the entire process, [Name]. We know it wasn't always easy, and we're grateful you stuck with us. It's reviews like yours that remind us why we do this work. All the best to you and your family.

4. Referral client:

[Name], we're so glad [referring person, if mentioned in review — otherwise skip] pointed you our way, and even happier that we could help. Thank you for trusting us with your matter, and thank you for taking the time to leave this review. We hope to be a resource for you anytime.

5. First-time client:

Welcome and thank you, [Name]. We know choosing an attorney for the first time can feel overwhelming, and we're honored you chose [Firm Name]. We hope you never need us again — but if you do, we're here. Thanks for the kind words.

Responding to Negative Reviews

Negative reviews are inevitable, even for excellent firms. How you respond matters more than the review itself.

The formula: Acknowledge + No Specifics + Offline Resolution

  1. Acknowledge — Show you take the feedback seriously
  2. No specifics — Never discuss case details, fees, timelines, or any factual assertions in a public response
  3. Offline resolution — Invite the person to contact you directly

Five Example Negative Review Responses

1. Unhappy with outcome:

[Name], we're sorry to hear you're unsatisfied with your experience. We take all client feedback seriously and would like the opportunity to discuss your concerns. Please contact our office at [phone number] so we can address this directly.

2. Fee dispute:

Thank you for your feedback, [Name]. We understand that legal costs can be a concern, and we're sorry if there was any confusion. We'd like to discuss this with you directly — please call our office at [phone number] at your convenience.

3. Communication complaint:

[Name], we appreciate you sharing this, and we apologize that our communication didn't meet your expectations. Keeping clients informed is a priority for us, and we'd like to learn more about where we fell short. Please reach out to us at [phone number] so we can discuss this.

4. Mistaken identity (wrong firm):

Thank you for your feedback. Based on the details you've described, we believe this review may have been intended for a different firm. We don't have a record matching this experience. If we're mistaken, please contact our office at [phone number] and we'll be happy to discuss.

5. Suspected competitor or fake review:

We don't have a record of a client by this name or matching this description. We take every review seriously, and we'd like to learn more. Please contact our office at [phone number] so we can look into this. If this review was posted in error, we'd appreciate the opportunity to resolve any confusion.

What to Never Say in a Response

Never Say Why
Any detail about the client's case Attorney-client confidentiality applies even if they shared details first
"You never paid your bill" Confirms a client relationship and introduces billing specifics
"We won your case, so we don't understand the complaint" Confirms outcome details and sounds defensive
"This is a fake review" Accusatory; handle through Google's flagging process instead
"We're sorry you feel that way" Dismissive non-apology that reads worse than silence
"Our records show..." Introduces factual claims that could become discovery material

The golden rule: Respond as if a judge, a prospective client, and a bar ethics committee are all reading your response. Because they might be.

Flagging Fake Reviews

If you receive a review that you believe is fake — posted by a competitor, a person who was never a client, or a bot — take these steps:

  1. Don't respond immediately. Assess first.
  2. Check your records. Can you identify the reviewer as a former client? If not, it may be fake.
  3. Flag the review through Google:
    • Open Google Maps and find your listing
    • Click on the review
    • Click the three-dot menu next to the review
    • Select "Flag as inappropriate"
    • Choose the appropriate reason (spam, fake, off-topic, etc.)
  4. If Google doesn't remove it (and they often don't on first try), submit a request through the Google Business Profile support portal with documentation explaining why the review is fraudulent.
  5. Post a calm, professional response (see template #5 above) while waiting for Google's decision.
  6. Document everything. If you suspect a coordinated competitor attack (multiple fake reviews in a short period), document the pattern and consider consulting an attorney who specializes in internet defamation.

Google removes reviews slowly and inconsistently. Don't rely on removal as your strategy. Your best defense is a strong body of authentic reviews that makes the occasional fake one irrelevant.

Chapter 6: Building Review Velocity — The Monthly System

Why Recency Matters More Than Total Count

Google's algorithm gives disproportionate weight to recent reviews. A firm with 50 reviews from the past three months will generally outrank a firm with 200 reviews, all from two years ago, in an otherwise comparable competitive situation.

This is because recency signals several things to Google:

  • The business is currently active and serving clients
  • The reviews reflect the current quality of service
  • The business is engaged with its audience

For your firm, this means a one-time review push has diminishing returns. What you need is a system — something that produces a steady, predictable flow of new reviews every month, indefinitely.

Setting a Monthly Review Goal by Firm Size

Your goal should be realistic and based on your client volume. Here are targets that firms in similar situations typically sustain:

Firm Size Monthly Closed Cases (Approx.) Monthly Review Target Implied Conversion Rate
Solo 5–15 2–4 ~25%
2–5 attorneys 15–40 5–10 ~25%
6–15 attorneys 40–100 10–25 ~25%
15+ attorneys 100+ 25+ ~25%

A 25% conversion rate (one review for every four requests) is a realistic, achievable target for firms using the 3-request sequence from Chapter 3. Some firms exceed this; few well-run systems fall below 15%.

Track your actual conversion rate. If it's below 15%, something in the process is broken — usually timing or the wrong person asking.

Building Review Requests Into Your Case Management Workflow

The review request can't be a separate, manual process someone remembers to do. It needs to be triggered by an event already in your workflow.

The trigger event: When a case status is changed to "Closed" (or your equivalent — "Settled," "Resolved," "Complete") in your case management system.

The process:

  1. Case marked as closed in CMS
  2. Within 48 hours, the assigned staff member sends Request 1 (email template)
  3. 7 days later, if no review received, send Request 2 (SMS)
  4. 10–14 days later, for high-value clients only, Request 3 (personal follow-up)

If your CMS supports task automation, create a task that auto-assigns to the review coordinator when a case status changes.

CRM Integration

Most legal CRMs can automate part of this process. Here's where to add the review request trigger in the three most common platforms:

Clio

  1. Go to Settings → Automations
  2. Create a new automation triggered by Matter status change → Closed
  3. Action: Send email using your Request 1 template
  4. Add a 7-day delayed action for Request 2 (if Clio's automation supports delays; otherwise, create a follow-up task)

Lawmatics

  1. Go to Automations → New Automation
  2. Trigger: Matter stage change → Closed/Won
  3. Add email step with Request 1 template
  4. Add a 7-day delay, then an SMS step with Request 2 template
  5. Lawmatics supports full drip sequences — you can build the entire 3-touch sequence as one automation

PracticePanther

  1. Go to Settings → Workflows
  2. Create a workflow triggered by Matter status → Closed
  3. Add a task: "Send review request email" assigned to your review coordinator
  4. Add a follow-up task 7 days later: "Send SMS review request if no review received"

If your CRM doesn't support automation, use a shared spreadsheet or task list. The coordinator checks the "closed cases" list weekly and sends requests manually. It's less elegant but equally effective if done consistently.

The Review Sprint: 12 to 50 Reviews in 90 Days

If your firm currently has a thin review profile (under 20 reviews), you can execute a structured sprint to build a credible base — without violating any policies.

Week 1–2: Batch outreach to past clients

  • Pull a list of clients from the past 2 years whose cases ended positively
  • Send 15 requests per week using the batch template from Chapter 3
  • Start with the most recent clients first (they're more likely to respond)

Week 3–6: Continue batch + begin new client system

  • Continue batch outreach at 15/week to past clients
  • Implement the standard 3-request sequence for all newly closed cases going forward
  • Follow up with SMS (Request 2) for any batch requests that didn't convert

Week 7–12: Transition to steady state

  • Batch requests should be winding down as you exhaust the list
  • New client requests are now the primary source
  • Target: by week 12, you should be generating reviews solely from the ongoing system

Expected results: If you start with 12 reviews and send 15 batch requests per week (at a 20% conversion rate = 3 new reviews/week) plus new client reviews, you should reach 40–50 total reviews within 90 days.

Important: Do not attempt to get 50 reviews in one week. Google's review filter is designed to catch sudden spikes. A steady 3–5 reviews per week looks natural. A sudden burst of 30 reviews in a week may result in many being silently filtered.

Chapter 7: Leveraging Reviews Beyond Google

Getting reviews is only half the value. The other half is using that social proof across every client touchpoint.

Using Reviews in Your Marketing

Reviews are the most credible marketing content you have — because you didn't write them. Here's where to deploy them:

Website

  • Homepage: Feature 2–3 recent reviews in a rotating testimonial section. Include the reviewer's first name, star rating, and the platform (e.g., "— Sarah M., Google Review").
  • Practice area pages: Show reviews relevant to that practice area on each page. A personal injury page should feature PI-specific reviews.
  • Dedicated testimonials page: Embed your full Google review feed or curate 15–20 of your best reviews.
  • Schema markup: Add Review schema to your website so star ratings appear in organic search results. Your web developer can implement this with structured data.

Proposals and Consultations

When you send a retainer agreement or consultation follow-up, include a section with 3–4 recent client reviews. This reinforces the client's decision to hire you during the critical consideration window.

Intake Packets

Add a page to your new client packet that features reviews from clients in similar situations. It reassures new clients that they made the right choice and sets the stage for them to eventually leave their own review.

Screenshot and Testimonial Best Practices

When repurposing reviews for marketing materials:

  1. Use screenshots of actual reviews rather than retyped text. Screenshots are more credible and harder to fabricate.
  2. Don't edit review text. Use it as-is. If a review contains a typo, leave the typo — it looks authentic.
  3. Include attribution — first name, last initial, and the platform. Never include full names without explicit consent.
  4. Add the required disclaimer where applicable. If a review discusses a specific result ("they got me $500,000"), your jurisdiction may require: "Results may vary. Every case is different." Check your state bar's advertising rules.
  5. Ask permission before using a client's review in paid advertising (social media ads, print ads). Organic reposts of public reviews on your own social media generally don't require permission, but paid amplification may cross a line depending on your jurisdiction.

Reviews in Social Media Content

Reviews make excellent social media content. Here are compliant ways to repurpose them:

  • Quote graphics: Take a compelling sentence from a review, overlay it on a branded background, and post it. Credit: "— Google Review."
  • Thank-you posts: "We're grateful for the kind words from our clients. Here's what [First Name] shared about working with us." Include the screenshot.
  • Video content: Read a review (or a compilation of review excerpts) in a short video. This works well on Instagram Reels and TikTok.
  • Stories/ephemeral content: Share review screenshots to Instagram or Facebook Stories with a "Thank you" sticker.

Post review content 2–3 times per month. Don't make every post a testimonial — mix it with educational content, firm news, and community involvement.

Review Content as an SEO Signal

When clients write detailed reviews, they naturally include keywords that improve your local search relevance. A review that says "Best personal injury attorney in Tampa — they handled my car accident case professionally" gives Google keyword signals that connect your business to those search terms.

You can't coach clients on what to write (that violates policy). But you can encourage detailed reviews by asking: "If you have a moment, a Google review about your experience would mean a lot. The more detail, the more it helps."

Detailed reviews also perform better in AI search contexts. When ChatGPT or Perplexity summarizes reviews, detailed text gives the AI more material to work with when generating recommendations.

Incorporating Reviews Into Your Intake Pitch

During intake consultations, mentioning your reviews is a natural trust-builder:

"We have over [X] five-star reviews on Google — I'd encourage you to check them out. A lot of our clients in situations similar to yours have shared their experience, and I think it'll give you a good sense of what working with us is like."

This isn't pushy. It's pointing a prospective client to the evidence that supports your pitch. It's also more credible than anything you could say about yourself.

A review system isn't just about generating reviews. It's also about protecting your profile and recovering when things go wrong.

Monthly Review Audit Checklist

Run this checklist on the first of every month:

  • Total review count (Google): ___
  • Reviews received this month: ___
  • Average star rating: ___
  • All reviews responded to? (Yes/No)
  • Average response time: ___ hours
  • Any new negative reviews? If yes, document and assess
  • Any reviews that disappeared? If yes, note which ones
  • Review request system functioning? (Are requests going out on schedule?)
  • GBP profile information still accurate? (Hours, phone, address)
  • Photos updated this quarter? (Yes/No)

This checklist takes 15 minutes. Delegate it to your review coordinator.

What to Do When a Review Disappears

Google removes reviews for various reasons, and doesn't always notify you:

  • Policy violation detected — Google's automated systems removed the review for violating their content policy
  • Spam filter — Google's algorithm determined the review was spam or fake (this can happen to legitimate reviews)
  • Account removed — The reviewer's Google account was suspended
  • Reviewer deleted it — The person chose to remove their own review

If a legitimate positive review disappears:

  1. Don't panic. It happens regularly and is usually temporary.
  2. Check if the reviewer's Google account still exists (search for their profile).
  3. If you have a relationship with the reviewer, you can politely mention that their review seems to have disappeared and ask if they'd be willing to re-post it. Don't pressure them.
  4. File a support request through Google Business Profile if you believe the removal was an error.
  5. Focus on generating new reviews to replace the lost one.

Never offer compensation to re-post a review.

Recovering From a Negative Review Spike

If you receive multiple negative reviews in a short period, assess the situation:

Is it a legitimate service problem? If multiple unrelated clients are expressing the same complaint (e.g., communication issues, billing confusion), you have an operational problem, not a review problem. Fix the service issue. Then respond to each review individually using the templates in Chapter 5.

Is it a coordinated attack? Signs of a fake review attack:

  • Multiple negative reviews from accounts with no review history
  • Reviews posted within hours of each other
  • Similar language or complaints across reviews
  • Reviewers who don't appear in your client records

Response to a coordinated attack:

  1. Flag every suspicious review through Google (see Chapter 5)
  2. Respond professionally to each one (don't accuse them of being fake in your response)
  3. Document the pattern: dates, reviewer names, content, screenshot everything
  4. Contact Google Business Profile support with your documentation
  5. If the attack continues or causes material business harm, consult an attorney who handles internet defamation

Recovery strategy: The best recovery from a negative review spike is an accelerated positive review flow. Increase your outreach cadence temporarily (not aggressively — stay within 15–20 requests per week). Fresh positive reviews will push negative ones down and restore your rating.

Handling Competitor Fake Review Attacks

Fake reviews from competitors are uncommon but real, particularly in competitive legal markets. Here's what you need to know:

  • Google's detection is imperfect. You may need to file multiple removal requests.
  • Don't retaliate. Never post fake reviews on a competitor's profile. This is unethical, potentially illegal, and easily detected.
  • Document obsessively. If you suspect a competitor, document the evidence: timing, patterns, account ages, cross-referencing with your client records.
  • Consider legal action as a last resort. An attorney specializing in internet law can send cease-and-desist letters and pursue defamation claims. This is expensive and should be reserved for cases where the fake reviews are causing demonstrable harm.

When to Consult an Attorney About Defamatory Reviews

Not every negative review is defamatory. In fact, most aren't. Defamation requires a false statement of fact (not opinion) that causes harm.

Likely defamatory (consult an attorney):

  • "This lawyer stole money from my trust account" (false factual claim)
  • "This firm was sanctioned by the bar" (false factual claim)
  • Reviews that reveal confidential information from your representation

Likely NOT defamatory (handle through response + monitoring):

  • "Worst lawyer ever" (opinion)
  • "They never returned my calls" (potentially subjective)
  • "Too expensive" (opinion)
  • "I didn't like the outcome" (opinion)

The line between opinion and defamation varies by jurisdiction. If a review contains specific false factual claims that are causing you to lose business, consult an attorney. For everything else, respond professionally and move on.

Appendix A: Direct Review Link Setup

Step-by-Step Instructions

Method 1: Using Google's Place ID Finder

  1. Open your web browser and navigate to: https://developers.google.com/maps/documentation/places/web-service/place-id
  2. Scroll down to the interactive map labeled "Place ID Finder"
  3. In the search box on the map, type your firm's exact name and city (e.g., "Smith Law Firm Tampa")
  4. Click on your firm in the search results
  5. A popup will appear with your Place ID — a long string starting with "ChIJ" (e.g., ChIJN1t_tDeuEmsRUsoyG83frY4)
  6. Copy this Place ID
  7. Construct your review URL: https://search.google.com/local/writereview?placeid=ChIJN1t_tDeuEmsRUsoyG83frY4 (replace with your actual Place ID)
  8. Test the URL in an incognito/private browser window
  9. Confirm it opens the Google review dialog for your firm

Method 2: Using Google Maps Directly

  1. Open Google Maps (maps.google.com)
  2. Search for your firm
  3. Click on your firm's listing
  4. In the URL bar, look for the portion after /place/ — you'll see your firm name and a long encoded string
  5. While on this page, click "Write a review"
  6. Copy the URL from the browser bar — this is your review URL
  7. Note: This method produces a longer URL. Use a URL shortener (bit.ly or similar) for cleaner links in emails and texts

Method 3: Using Google Business Profile Manager

  1. Log into business.google.com
  2. On your dashboard, look for the "Get more reviews" card or "Share review form" button
  3. Click it — Google generates a short review link for you
  4. Copy and save this link

Regardless of method, always:

  • Test the link in incognito mode before using it
  • Test on both desktop and mobile
  • Save the link somewhere accessible to everyone who sends review requests
  • If the link ever stops working (rare but possible after GBP changes), regenerate it

Appendix G: Glossary

Term Definition
GBP (Google Business Profile) Google's free business listing tool (formerly Google My Business). Controls how your firm appears in Google Search and Maps.
Map Pack The block of three local business results with a map that appears at the top of Google search results for local queries. Also called the "Local Pack" or "3-Pack."
Review velocity The rate at which new reviews are posted over time. Measured as reviews per week or per month.
Review gating The prohibited practice of pre-screening clients and only directing satisfied ones to leave public reviews.
Place ID A unique identifier Google assigns to every business location. Used to construct direct review links.
NAP Name, Address, Phone number. Consistency of NAP across the web is a local SEO ranking factor.
Local SEO Search engine optimization focused on improving visibility in geographically relevant search results.
Schema markup Structured data code added to a website that helps search engines understand content. Review schema can display star ratings in search results.
Conversion rate (reviews) The percentage of review requests that result in a posted review. Formula: reviews received ÷ requests sent.
NPS (Net Promoter Score) A survey methodology that measures client satisfaction on a 0–10 scale. Using NPS as a review gating filter is a policy violation.
AI Overview Google's AI-generated summary that appears at the top of some search results. Pulls from multiple sources including reviews.
Drip sequence An automated series of messages sent at predetermined intervals. Used for the 3-request review sequence.
Star rating The average of all review scores (1–5 stars) on a platform.
Review filter Google's automated system that removes reviews it suspects are fake, spam, or policy-violating. Can affect legitimate reviews.
Defamation A false statement of fact published to a third party that causes harm to reputation. Legal standard varies by jurisdiction.
Service area business A GBP listing type for businesses that serve clients at the client's location rather than at a fixed office.
CMS (Case Management System) Software used by law firms to manage cases, clients, documents, and workflows (e.g., Clio, PracticePanther).
CRM (Client Relationship Management) Software for managing client interactions and marketing automation. Some legal CMS tools include CRM features.
Retargeting Online advertising that shows ads to people who previously visited your website. Reviews in retargeting ads increase click-through rates.
