Chapter 1: Why Local Search Is Winner-Take-All

How the Google Map Pack Works

When someone searches "personal injury lawyer near me" or "divorce attorney Tampa," Google shows two types of results:

  1. The Map Pack (also called the Local Pack or 3-Pack) — three business listings with a map at the top of the page
  2. Organic results — the traditional blue links below

The Map Pack dominates. Industry research consistently shows that the Map Pack captures the majority of clicks for local-intent searches. The top three positions in the Map Pack receive dramatically more engagement than anything below them. Position four and beyond? Buried behind a "More places" click that most searchers never make.

For law firms, this is the game. Someone in crisis — arrested, injured, served with divorce papers — searches on their phone, sees three firms in the Map Pack, and calls one. If you're not in those three slots, you functionally don't exist for that search.

Here's what makes this winner-take-all: there are only three spots, and Google rotates them less than you'd think. The same firms tend to dominate the Map Pack for months or years at a time. Once you earn a top position, it compounds — more clicks lead to more calls, more calls lead to more reviews, more reviews reinforce your ranking.

The Three Ranking Factors

Google has publicly stated that local search rankings are determined by three factors:

1. Relevance

How well your business profile matches what the searcher is looking for. If someone searches "criminal defense lawyer" and your Google Business Profile lists "Criminal Defense Attorney" as your primary category, you're relevant. If your profile says "Law Firm" with no specifics, you're not.

Relevance is driven by:

  • Your primary and secondary GBP categories
  • Keywords in your business description
  • Services listed on your profile
  • Content on your website that matches the search query

2. Distance

How close your business is to the searcher or the location they specified. You can't change where your office is, but you can influence this factor through:

  • Having a verified physical address in your GBP
  • Creating location-specific pages on your website for each area you serve
  • Building citations that consistently list your address

Distance is the one factor you have the least control over, which is why the other two matter even more.

3. Prominence

How well-known and trusted your business is. Google measures prominence through:

  • Reviews: Volume, velocity, recency, rating, and response rate
  • Citations: How many reputable directories list your firm with consistent information
  • Backlinks: Links from other websites to yours
  • Website authority: Overall domain strength
  • Engagement: Click-through rates, calls, direction requests from your GBP

Prominence is where most of your work goes and where most law firms fail. A firm with 200 reviews, 50 accurate citations, and a strong website will outrank a firm 2 miles closer to the searcher almost every time.

Why Most Law Firms Are Leaving This Wide Open

The legal industry has a massive local SEO gap. Here's why:

Most firms delegate marketing to someone who doesn't understand local search. The partner's nephew "does marketing." The receptionist updates the website. An agency was hired three years ago and nobody checks what they're doing.

Law firm websites are built for ego, not search. Beautiful homepage with a skyline photo, partner bios, and "Results-Driven Representation." Zero location pages. Zero practice-area-plus-city pages. The site looks impressive and ranks for nothing.

Nobody owns the Google Business Profile. It was claimed during setup and hasn't been touched since. No posts, no photos, no Q&As, old hours, wrong categories.

Citations are a mess. The firm moved offices two years ago. Half the internet still lists the old address. There are three duplicate GBP listings from previous partners. Avvo says one phone number, Justia says another.

Reviews are an afterthought. The firm has 23 reviews, most from 2022. The competitor across the street has 180 reviews from the last 12 months. Guess who Google trusts more.

This is your opportunity. Most of your competitors are not doing this work. The firms that execute the playbook in this guide will dominate — not because the tactics are secret, but because the consistency required eliminates most competition.

AI Search and Local Signals

Google's AI Overviews, Bing's Copilot, and ChatGPT search are reshaping how people find information. But here's what matters for local search: AI search tools still rely heavily on Google Business Profile data, review signals, and structured local information.

When an AI generates a response to "best personal injury lawyer in Tampa," it pulls from:

  • Google Business Profile data (categories, descriptions, reviews)
  • Structured data on your website (schema markup)
  • Citation consistency across the web
  • Review volume, recency, and sentiment

The firms that show up in AI-generated answers are the same firms that have strong local SEO fundamentals. There's no separate "AI SEO" strategy. The playbook you're holding works for traditional search and AI search simultaneously.

What is changing: AI search tends to surface fewer results, making the winner-take-all dynamic even more extreme. Instead of a 3-pack, AI might recommend one or two firms. The bar is higher, but the reward is greater.

Chapter 2: Google Business Profile — Your Most Important Local Asset

Your Google Business Profile (GBP) is the single most important asset in local search. It's free, it's directly controlled by you, and it's the primary data source Google uses for Map Pack rankings. If you do nothing else in this playbook, do this chapter.

Claim and Verify Your GBP

If your firm already has a claimed and verified GBP, skip to the next section. If not, follow these steps:

  1. Go to business.google.com
  2. Sign in with a Google account your firm controls (use a firm-owned Google Workspace account, not a personal Gmail)
  3. Search for your firm name. If it appears, click it and select "Claim this business." If it doesn't appear, click "Add your business to Google."
  4. Enter your exact legal business name — no keyword stuffing (e.g., "Smith & Associates" not "Smith & Associates Personal Injury Lawyers Tampa")
  5. Select your primary business category (see the next section)
  6. Enter your physical office address. If you serve clients at their location, you can also set service areas — but you must have a physical address.
  7. Enter your phone number and website URL
  8. Choose a verification method. Google typically offers:
    • Postcard by mail (5–14 days) — most common
    • Phone verification — sometimes available for established businesses
    • Video verification — Google may ask you to record a video of your office, signage, and surroundings
    • Email verification — less common, usually for businesses with a verified domain
  9. Complete verification. Do not edit your business name or address until verification is complete — changes can restart the process.

Important: If you find an existing listing that someone else claimed (a former partner, an old agency), you can request ownership through Google's "Request Access" flow. This can take 7–14 days.

Business Description

Your GBP business description can be up to 750 characters. This is prime real estate for relevance signals.

Formula:

[Firm name] is a [primary practice area] law firm serving [city/region]. Our attorneys handle [practice area 1], [practice area 2], and [practice area 3] cases for [client type] throughout [geographic area]. With [differentiator], we [value proposition]. Call [phone] for a [free/confidential] consultation.

Example (685 characters):

Carter & Simmons is a personal injury law firm serving Tampa and Hillsborough County. Our trial attorneys handle car accidents, truck accidents, slip and fall injuries, and wrongful death cases for individuals and families throughout the Tampa Bay area. With over 40 years of combined courtroom experience, we fight to recover maximum compensation for our clients — and you pay nothing unless we win. Our firm has recovered millions in verdicts and settlements for injury victims across Florida. We are available 24/7 for emergencies. Call (813) 555-0100 for a free, no-obligation case evaluation.

Rules:

  • Use your practice area keywords naturally — don't stuff
  • Include your city and region
  • Mention specific case types
  • Include your phone number
  • Include a call to action
  • No URLs allowed in the description
  • No all-caps, no promotional language like "best" or "#1"

Q&A Section

The Q&A section on your GBP is public — anyone can ask a question, and anyone can answer. If you don't seed it with your own Q&As, random people (or competitors) will.

Seed your Q&A with 10 questions and answers. Here are examples for a personal injury firm (adapt to your practice area):

Q1: Do I have to pay anything upfront for a personal injury case? A1: No. We handle all personal injury cases on a contingency fee basis. You pay nothing unless we win your case. There are no upfront costs or out-of-pocket expenses.

Q2: How long do I have to file a personal injury lawsuit in [state]? A2: In [state], the statute of limitations for most personal injury cases is [X] years from the date of injury. However, certain cases have shorter deadlines, so we recommend contacting us as soon as possible to protect your rights.

Q3: What should I do immediately after a car accident? A3: First, seek medical attention — even if you feel fine, some injuries take days to appear. Call the police to file a report. Exchange information with the other driver. Take photos of the scene, damage, and any visible injuries. Then contact a personal injury attorney before speaking with the other driver's insurance company.

Q4: How much is my personal injury case worth? A4: Every case is different. The value depends on the severity of your injuries, medical expenses, lost wages, pain and suffering, and the available insurance coverage. We offer free case evaluations and can give you a realistic assessment after reviewing your situation.

Q5: Do I have to go to court for my personal injury case? A5: Most personal injury cases settle without going to trial. However, our attorneys are experienced trial lawyers who are prepared to take your case to court if the insurance company won't offer a fair settlement.

Q6: What types of cases does your firm handle? A6: We handle car accidents, truck accidents, motorcycle accidents, slip and fall injuries, premises liability, wrongful death, medical malpractice, and product liability cases throughout [city] and [region].

Q7: Can I still file a claim if I was partially at fault for the accident? A7: In [state], you can still recover compensation even if you were partially at fault under the [comparative/contributory] negligence rule. Your compensation may be reduced by your percentage of fault. Contact us for a free evaluation of your specific situation.

Q8: How long does a typical personal injury case take? A8: Straightforward cases often settle within 6–12 months. More complex cases involving severe injuries, multiple parties, or litigation can take 1–3 years. We work to resolve your case as efficiently as possible while maximizing your recovery.

Q9: What if the insurance company already contacted me? A9: Do not give a recorded statement or sign anything from the insurance company before speaking with an attorney. Insurance adjusters are trained to minimize payouts. Contact us first — we'll handle all communication with the insurance company on your behalf.

Q10: Where is your office located, and do you offer free parking? A10: Our office is located at [address] in [city]. We offer free parking for all clients directly in front of our building. We also offer virtual consultations by phone or video for clients who prefer not to travel.

How to post Q&As:

  1. Search for your firm on Google (while logged into the Google account that owns your GBP)
  2. On your listing, find the "Ask a question" button
  3. Post each question from your business account
  4. Answer each question from the same account — your answer will show as the "Owner's answer" and be pinned to the top

GBP Posts

Google Business Profile Posts are short updates that appear on your listing. They signal to Google that your business is active and give searchers more reasons to engage.

Post types:

  • Update: General news, tips, firm updates
  • Offer: Promotions (e.g., "Free Case Evaluation This Month")
  • Event: Upcoming events with date/time
  • Product: Highlight specific services

Posting cadence: 1 post per week minimum.

Posts expire from prominent display after 6 months, though they remain accessible. Consistency matters more than volume.

What to write about:

  • Case type spotlights: "What to do after a slip and fall in [city]"
  • Legal tips: "3 mistakes to avoid after a car accident"
  • Firm news: New attorney, office expansion, award
  • Community involvement: Sponsorships, charity events
  • Seasonal content: "Holiday DUI checkpoints in [county] — know your rights"
  • FAQs: Turn common client questions into posts

Post format:

  • 150–300 words
  • Include a relevant photo (not stock)
  • Add a call-to-action button: "Call now," "Learn more," or "Book online"
  • Include your target keywords naturally
  • Link to a relevant page on your website

Attributes, Messaging, and Website Link

Attributes: Fill out every applicable attribute in your GBP. These include:

  • Accessibility (wheelchair accessible entrance, restroom)
  • Service options (online appointments, onsite services)
  • Highlights (veteran-led, women-led)
  • Languages spoken
  • Payment methods accepted

Check your attributes quarterly — Google adds new ones regularly.

Messaging: Enable Google messaging if your firm can respond within a few hours. If you can't commit to fast response times, leave it off — slow responses hurt your profile.

To enable: GBP dashboard → Messaging → Turn on

Website link: Ensure your GBP links to your homepage or a dedicated landing page — not a random internal page. If you have practice-area-specific GBP listings (multi-practitioner firms), link each to the corresponding practice area page.

Chapter 3: Citations — The Foundation Nobody Builds

What Citations Are

A citation is any online mention of your law firm's name, address, and phone number (NAP). Citations appear on business directories, legal directories, social platforms, data aggregators, and industry-specific websites.

Citations serve two purposes:

  1. They validate your business information to Google. The more places that list your firm with consistent NAP data, the more confident Google is that your business is real, located where you say it is, and trustworthy.
  2. They provide direct traffic. Some directories — particularly legal-specific ones like Avvo and FindLaw — drive actual client calls and form submissions.

Why NAP Consistency Matters

NAP = Name, Address, Phone number.

Every citation must list your firm's NAP identically. Not approximately. Identically.

Inconsistencies that hurt you:

  • "Smith & Associates LLC" vs. "Smith and Associates" vs. "Smith & Associates, LLC"
  • "123 Main Street, Suite 200" vs. "123 Main St #200" vs. "123 Main St., Ste. 200"
  • "(813) 555-0100" vs. "813-555-0100" vs. "8135550100"
  • Old addresses from previous office locations
  • Old phone numbers from previous phone systems
  • Different business names from rebranding or partner changes

Pick one standard format and use it everywhere:

Field Standard Format
Business name Exact legal name as on GBP
Street address Match USPS standard format
Suite/unit "Suite 200" (not "Ste." or "#")
City, State, ZIP Full state name or consistent abbreviation
Phone (XXX) XXX-XXXX
Website https://www.yourfirm.com (with or without www — pick one)

How to Audit Current Citations

Before building new citations, audit what already exists. Mismatched listings do more harm than no listing at all.

Manual audit process:

  1. Search Google for your exact firm name in quotes: "Smith & Associates LLC"
  2. Search for your firm name + city: "Smith & Associates" Tampa
  3. Search for your phone number: "(813) 555-0100"
  4. Search for your old phone number (if applicable): "(813) 555-0199"
  5. Search for your old address (if you moved): "456 Old Street Tampa"

For each result, record in a spreadsheet:

  • Directory name
  • URL of your listing
  • Business name as listed
  • Address as listed
  • Phone number as listed
  • Whether it's correct or needs updating
  • Login credentials (if you created an account)

Free tools that help:

  • Moz Local Check (moz.com/local) — scans major directories for your listing
  • BrightLocal Free Citation Audit — shows where you're listed and flags inconsistencies
  • Google search — the manual approach above catches what tools miss

How to Fix Inconsistencies

Work through your audit spreadsheet and fix every incorrect listing:

  1. Log in and edit directly — if you have an account on the directory
  2. Claim the listing — most directories let you claim an unclaimed listing and then edit it
  3. Contact support — for directories that don't allow self-service edits, email their support team with your correction request. Include proof of business (utility bill, business license, or GBP screenshot)
  4. Submit to data aggregators — four data aggregators feed information to hundreds of smaller directories:
    • Data Axle (dataaxle.com) — submit updates directly
    • Neustar Localeze (neustarlocaleze.biz) — submit updates directly
    • Foursquare (foursquare.com) — claim and update
    • Factual (now Foursquare) — merged with Foursquare

Updating the four aggregators can fix dozens of downstream citations automatically. Start there.

Duplicate Suppression

Duplicate GBP listings are common for law firms — especially after partner changes, office moves, or rebrands.

How to find duplicates:

  1. Search Google Maps for your firm name
  2. Search for your address — do multiple listings appear?
  3. Search for variations of your firm name
  4. Check for individual attorney GBPs that duplicate the firm listing

How to remove duplicates:

  1. If you own the duplicate listing: log into GBP → select the duplicate → click "Remove this listing" → "Mark as duplicate" or "Close this business"
  2. If you don't own it: use the "Suggest an edit" feature on Google Maps → "Close or remove" → "Duplicate of another place"
  3. For persistent duplicates: use Google Business Profile support (support.google.com/business) and provide documentation

Important: Don't remove individual attorney GBPs if they serve a purpose. In multi-attorney firms, individual GBPs for each attorney can be powerful — but they need to be managed, not duplicated.

Chapter 4: Location Pages — How Google Knows Where You Serve

Why Location Pages Beat a Single Homepage

Your homepage targets one location — where your office is. But you probably serve clients across an entire metro area, county, or region. Without location pages, Google has no reason to show you for searches in those other areas.

A location page is a dedicated page on your website for a specific [practice area] + [city] combination. When someone searches "car accident lawyer Brandon FL," Google looks for a page that specifically addresses car accident representation in Brandon. If your competitor has that page and you don't, they win.

This is not about tricking Google. Location pages provide genuinely useful information to potential clients in those areas — local court details, area-specific information, and clear messaging that "yes, we serve your city."

Page Structure: One Page Per Combination

Create one page for each combination of:

  • Practice area (your top 3–5 revenue generators)
  • City/neighborhood (the 5–15 areas where you want clients)

Example matrix for a personal injury firm in the Tampa Bay area:

Tampa St. Petersburg Clearwater Brandon Riverview
Car Accidents ✓ ✓ ✓ ✓ ✓
Truck Accidents ✓ ✓ ✓ ✓ ✓

Wrongful Death ✓ ✓ ✓ ✓ ✓

That's 20 location pages. Each one is a new opportunity to rank for a specific local search.

Don't go overboard. Start with your top 3 practice areas × top 5 cities = 15 pages. Expand from there based on search volume and competition.

Exact Page Template

Use this template for every location page. Customize the content for each city — do not copy-paste the same content with city names swapped.

Title Tag Formula

[Practice Area] Lawyer in [City], [State] | [Firm Name]

Example: Car Accident Lawyer in Brandon, FL | Carter & Simmons

Character limit: 50–60 characters. If your firm name makes it too long, abbreviate or drop it.

H1 Formula

[City] [Practice Area] Lawyer

Example: Brandon Car Accident Lawyer

Meta Description Formula

Need a [practice area] lawyer in [city]? [Firm name] has [experience detail]. Free consultation. Call [phone].

Example: Need a car accident lawyer in Brandon? Carter & Simmons has 40+ years of trial experience. Free consultation. Call (813) 555-0100.

Character limit: 150–160 characters.

URL Structure

/[practice-area]-lawyer-[city]-[state]/

Example: /car-accident-lawyer-brandon-fl/

Page Sections

Each location page should contain these sections in order:

1. Hero section with H1 (50–100 words) Immediately address the reader's situation. Mention the city. Include a call to action.

2. Why you need a [practice area] attorney in [city] (150–200 words) Discuss local-specific factors: local courts, local traffic patterns (for auto accidents), local hazards, or area-specific legal nuances.

3. How we help [city] clients (150–200 words) Your firm's approach, experience, and what sets you apart. Specific to this practice area.

4. Common [practice area] cases in [city] (150–200 words) List specific case types. For car accidents: intersection accidents, highway accidents, rideshare accidents, etc. Reference specific local roads or landmarks naturally.

5. What compensation can you recover (150–200 words) Types of damages: medical bills, lost wages, pain and suffering, etc. State-specific statute of limitations.

6. Our process / what to expect (100–150 words) Numbered steps from initial call to resolution.

7. Serving [city] and surrounding areas (100 words) List the neighborhoods and nearby cities you also serve. This adds geographic relevance.

8. Call to action (50 words) Phone number, contact form, free consultation offer.

9. FAQ section (3–5 questions, 50–100 words each) Practice-area and location-specific questions. Use <h3> tags for each question.

Word Count Target

1,200–1,800 words per page. Long enough to be comprehensive, short enough that it's not padded.

Internal Linking

  • Link each location page to your main practice area page
  • Link each location page to your homepage
  • Link between related location pages (e.g., "We also serve nearby [other city]")
  • Link to your contact page
  • Use descriptive anchor text: "our Tampa personal injury attorneys" not "click here"

What NOT to Do

Do not create doorway pages. A doorway page is a thin, duplicate page where you only swap out the city name. Google penalizes this. Each location page must have unique, substantive content specific to that city.

Signs of a doorway page:

  • The only difference between pages is the city name
  • Content is under 500 words
  • No location-specific information (courts, roads, landmarks)
  • The page exists solely for SEO with no value to the reader

Other mistakes to avoid:

  • Don't create pages for cities where you have no real connection or ability to serve
  • Don't hide location pages from your site navigation — they should be accessible
  • Don't use the same meta description on multiple location pages
  • Don't stuff keywords: "Tampa car accident lawyer, Tampa auto accident attorney, Tampa vehicle crash lawyer" — write naturally
  • Don't use AI-generated content without significant human editing and local knowledge added

How to Get Pages Indexed Fast

After publishing new location pages:

  1. Submit each URL in Google Search Console:

    • Go to search.google.com/search-console
    • Enter the new page URL in the top search bar ("Inspect any URL")
    • Click "Request Indexing"

  2. Add internal links immediately:

    • Link to the new page from your homepage, practice area pages, or blog posts
    • Google discovers new pages by crawling links from existing indexed pages

  3. Submit an updated sitemap:

    • Update your XML sitemap to include the new pages
    • In Search Console, go to Sitemaps → submit your sitemap URL

  4. Share on social media:

    • Post the page URL on your Facebook business page and LinkedIn
    • Social shares can accelerate Google's crawling

Typical indexing time: 2–14 days. If a page isn't indexed after 2 weeks, re-request in Search Console and check for crawl errors.

Chapter 5: Reviews — The Ranking Signal You Control

Why Reviews Matter for Map Pack

Reviews are the most visible trust signal in local search and one of the strongest ranking factors for the Map Pack. They impact rankings in three ways:

  1. Volume: More reviews = more data for Google to trust
  2. Velocity: Consistent new reviews signal an active business
  3. Rating: Higher average ratings improve click-through rates and rankings
  4. Recency: Recent reviews matter more than old ones
  5. Keywords in reviews: When clients mention practice areas or locations in their reviews, it reinforces your relevance

A firm with 15 reviews from 2022 will lose to a firm with 100 reviews from the last 12 months — even if both have 5-star averages.

Review benchmarks by market size:

Market Size Competitive Review Count Dominant Review Count
Small city (<100K) 50+ 100+
Mid-size metro (100K–500K) 100+ 200+
Large metro (500K+) 200+ 400+

Review Request Sequence

The key to getting reviews consistently: ask systematically, make it easy, and follow up once.

When to Ask

Ask within 24–48 hours of a positive resolution or milestone:

  • Case settled or won
  • Positive outcome at hearing
  • Client expressed gratitude
  • Successful closing (real estate)
  • Estate plan completed

Never ask during an active, stressful phase of a case. Timing matters.

Step 1: Initial Email Request

Subject: Quick favor, [Client First Name]?

Body:

Hi [Client First Name],

It was a pleasure working with you on your [case type] matter.
If you had a positive experience with our firm, would you mind
leaving us a brief Google review? It takes about 60 seconds
and helps other people in similar situations find us.

Here's the direct link:
[Your Google Review Link]

A sentence or two is plenty — no need to write an essay.

Thank you,
[Attorney Name]
[Firm Name]
[Phone Number]

Step 2: SMS Request (same day or next day)

Hi [First Name], this is [Attorney First Name] from [Firm Name].
Thank you again for trusting us with your [case type]. If you
have a minute, a Google review would mean a lot to our team:
[Short Review Link]

Step 3: Follow-Up (5–7 days later, only if no review posted)

Subject: Following up — [Firm Name]

Body:

Hi [Client First Name],

Just a quick follow-up on my earlier note. If you have 60 seconds,
a Google review would be a huge help for our firm:

[Your Google Review Link]

No worries if you're too busy — we appreciate you either way.

Best,
[Attorney Name]

Rules:

  • Never send more than one follow-up
  • Never incentivize reviews (no discounts, gifts, or rewards — this violates Google's policies and FTC guidelines)
  • Never ask only satisfied clients and filter out dissatisfied ones (this is "review gating" — see below)

How to Get Your Google Review Link

  1. Go to Google Maps and search for your firm
  2. Click on your listing
  3. Click "Write a review"
  4. Copy the URL from your browser's address bar

Shorter method:

  1. Go to your GBP dashboard at business.google.com
  2. Click "Ask for reviews" (or "Get more reviews")
  3. Copy the short link provided

Create a vanity redirect: Set up a simple redirect like yourfirm.com/review that points to your Google review link. This is easier to share verbally and in print.

Response Templates

Respond to every review — positive and negative. Google has confirmed that responding to reviews improves your local ranking.

Positive Review Response Templates

Template 1 — Short:

Thank you for the kind words, [Name]. It was a privilege to
represent you, and we're glad we could help. We wish you
all the best.

Template 2 — Practice area mention (adds keyword relevance):

Thank you, [Name]. [Practice area] cases can be stressful,
and we're happy we could achieve a positive outcome for you.
Your recommendation means a lot to our team at [Firm Name].

Template 3 — Location mention:

[Name], thank you for your generous review. Serving clients
in [city] and throughout [region] is what drives our team.
We appreciate your trust in [Firm Name].

Rotate templates. Don't post the same response to every review.

Negative Review Response Templates

Template 1 — Professional and brief:

[Name], we're sorry to hear about your experience. Client
satisfaction is important to us. Please contact our office
at [phone] so we can discuss your concerns directly.

Template 2 — Factual correction (use carefully):

[Name], we take all feedback seriously. We'd like to address
your concerns privately. Please call us at [phone] or email
[email] so we can work toward a resolution.

Rules for negative reviews:

  • Never argue in public
  • Never disclose case details or confirm the person is a client (attorney-client privilege)
  • Keep it short — lengthy responses look defensive
  • Take the conversation offline
  • If a review violates Google's policies (fake, spam, conflict of interest), flag it for removal through your GBP dashboard

Review Gating Warning

Review gating is asking clients about their experience first, then only sending the review link to satisfied clients. This is explicitly prohibited by Google and the FTC.

Examples of review gating:

  • Sending a survey first, then only asking 4–5 star respondents to leave a Google review
  • Using software that filters negative feedback away from Google
  • Having a "How was your experience?" page that redirects happy clients to Google and unhappy clients to an internal form

If Google detects review gating, they can remove your reviews and penalize your listing. Don't do it.

What you can do: Ask all clients for reviews. If someone has a negative experience, you can (and should) try to resolve it directly — but you cannot prevent them from reviewing you.

Set review goals based on your market and current position:

Timeframe Goal
Month 1–3 5 new reviews per month (build the system)
Month 4–6 8–10 new reviews per month (refine and scale)
Month 7–12 10–15 new reviews per month (consistent cadence)
Year 2+ Maintain 10+ new reviews per month

Track monthly:

  • Total review count
  • Average rating
  • Number of new reviews this month
  • Response rate (should be 100%)

Chapter 6: Schema Markup — Speak Google's Language

What Schema Markup Is (Plain English)

Schema markup is code you add to your website that tells Google exactly what your business is, where it's located, who works there, and what services you offer. Think of it as a structured label for your business — instead of Google guessing from your page content, you're spelling it out in a format Google can read perfectly.

Schema doesn't change how your website looks to visitors. It's invisible code embedded in your page's HTML. But it can dramatically improve how Google understands and displays your business in search results — including rich snippets with star ratings, FAQ dropdowns, and enhanced business information.

Three Schemas Every Law Firm Needs

1. LocalBusiness / LegalService Schema

This tells Google your firm's name, address, phone number, hours, and practice areas. It goes on your homepage (and can go on location pages with modified address/area information).

2. Attorney / Person Schema

This tells Google about individual attorneys — their name, credentials, job title, and association with your firm. It goes on attorney bio pages.

3. FAQPage Schema

This tells Google that a page contains a list of questions and answers, enabling FAQ rich snippets in search results. It goes on any page with an FAQ section — including location pages.

Implementation Methods

Method 1: Direct code injection (recommended) Paste JSON-LD code into the <head> section of your pages. Most CMS platforms let you add custom code to the head.

Method 2: WordPress plugins

  • Yoast SEO (free) — has basic schema support
  • Rank Math (free) — has robust schema support
  • Schema Pro (paid) — dedicated schema plugin

Method 3: Google Tag Manager Add JSON-LD via a Custom HTML tag fired on specific pages. Good if you don't want to edit page templates.

JSON-LD is the recommended format. It's the format Google prefers, it's the easiest to implement, and it doesn't require modifying your visible page content.

How to Test Your Schema

  1. Go to Google's Rich Results Test: search.google.com/test/rich-results
  2. Enter your page URL or paste your code
  3. Click "Test URL" or "Test Code"
  4. Review the results — Google will flag any errors or warnings
  5. Fix any issues and re-test

Also use the Schema Markup Validator at validator.schema.org for general schema validation.

Common errors:

  • Missing required fields (name, address for LocalBusiness)
  • Invalid URL formats
  • Mismatched data (schema says one address, page content says another)
  • Incorrect nesting of objects

Chapter 7: Monthly Maintenance

What Decays Without Maintenance

Local SEO is not set-and-forget. Here's what degrades over time:

  • GBP posts expire from prominent display after 6 months
  • Photos get stale — Google favors fresh visual content
  • Reviews stop if you stop asking — and competitors keep building theirs
  • Citations get overwritten by data aggregators with old information
  • Location pages become outdated if local information changes
  • Schema can break during website updates or CMS upgrades
  • Google changes its algorithm, features, and GBP interface regularly

Monthly Checklist (30 Minutes)

Do this every month without fail:

  • Post 4 GBP updates (one per week — batch-write them monthly)
  • Upload 2–3 new photos to your GBP
  • Check and respond to all new reviews (should be doing this weekly, but review monthly for any missed)
  • Check GBP Q&A for new questions from the public — answer within 24 hours
  • Review GBP insights — note trends in searches, views, and actions
  • Send review requests to all clients with positive outcomes this month
  • Check your GBP listing on Google Maps — make sure nothing changed (Google sometimes auto-edits listings based on user suggestions)
  • Check for new Google Business Profile features — Google regularly adds new fields and features

Quarterly Checklist

Every 3 months, add these tasks:

  • Audit your top 10 citations — spot-check that NAP is still correct on Yelp, Avvo, Facebook, Bing, Apple, and 5 others
  • Update GBP business description if anything has changed (new practice areas, new attorneys, new office)
  • Review and update GBP services section — add new services, remove discontinued ones
  • Check GBP categories — are there new categories that better match your practice?
  • Review location page content — update any outdated information (court addresses, statute of limitations changes, new local developments)
  • Test schema markup on all key pages using the Rich Results Test
  • Review Google Search Console for crawl errors, indexing issues, or manual actions
  • Check competitor GBP listings — what are the top 3 competitors doing that you're not?
  • Update GBP attributes — Google adds new attributes regularly
  • Review photo quality — remove any low-quality or outdated photos

Annual Checklist

Once per year, do a comprehensive review:

  • Full citation audit — re-check all 50 sources from Chapter 3
  • Review and rewrite GBP business description — refresh with current messaging
  • Audit all location pages — are they still accurate? Are there new cities to target?
  • Review overall local SEO performance — compare this year's Map Pack rankings, review count, and website traffic to last year
  • Update schema markup — check for new schema types or properties relevant to law firms
  • Refresh all photo content — new headshots, new office photos, new team photos
  • Evaluate practice area strategy — should primary/secondary GBP categories change based on business goals?
  • Review and update all templates — review request emails, SMS templates, response templates
  • Check for Google algorithm updates — what changed this year and how should your strategy adapt?
  • Set goals for next year — review count target, new location pages, new citation sources

Chapter 7 Action Items:

  • Block 30 minutes on your calendar for the first of every month — "Local SEO Maintenance"
  • Block 2 hours at the start of each quarter for the quarterly review
  • Block a half-day annually for the comprehensive review
  • Create a shared document or spreadsheet to track monthly/quarterly/annual tasks

Sudden ranking drop:

  • Check for Google algorithm updates (search "Google algorithm update [month] [year]")

  • Check if a competitor got a surge of reviews or new citations

  • Do not create a new listing — fix the existing one

  • They may have hired a local SEO agency — you need to match their effort

Appendix F: Glossary

Citation: Any online mention of your business name, address, and phone number (NAP).

Doorway Page: A thin, low-quality page created primarily for search engines rather than users. Google penalizes these.

GBP (Google Business Profile): Google's free business listing platform, formerly known as Google My Business (GMB).

JSON-LD: JavaScript Object Notation for Linked Data — the format Google recommends for adding schema markup to web pages.

Local Pack / Map Pack / 3-Pack: The top three local business results shown with a map in Google search results for local-intent queries.

NAP: Name, Address, Phone number — the three core pieces of business information that must be consistent across all citations.

Organic Results: The traditional "blue link" search results that appear below the Map Pack.

Prominence: One of Google's three local ranking factors — how well-known and trusted your business is online.

Relevance: One of Google's three local ranking factors — how well your business profile matches the search query.

Review Gating: The prohibited practice of screening customer satisfaction before directing them to a review platform, filtering out unhappy customers.

Review Velocity: The rate at which new reviews are posted — measured in reviews per month.

Rich Snippet: Enhanced search results that display additional information (star ratings, FAQs, business hours) pulled from schema markup.

Schema Markup: Structured data code added to web pages that helps search engines understand the content. Uses vocabulary from schema.org.

Service Area: The geographic region your business serves, as defined in your Google Business Profile.

Structured Data: Information organized in a standardized format that search engines can easily parse. Schema markup is a type of structured data.

When to Hire Help vs. DIY

DIY this playbook if:

  • You have a staff member who can dedicate 2–4 hours/week for the first month, then 30–60 minutes/month
  • Your firm is in a small to mid-size market with moderate competition
  • You're willing to follow the system exactly as written
  • You have basic comfort with websites and online tools

Hire help if:

  • Nobody at your firm has the time or inclination to do this consistently
  • You're in a highly competitive metro market and need to move fast
  • You've tried DIY before and it stalled after month one
  • You want someone accountable for results, not just activity
  • Your website needs significant technical work (page speed, mobile optimization, CMS migration)

What to look for in a local SEO provider:

  • They specialize in local search (not general "digital marketing")
  • They can show you current client results in Map Pack rankings
  • They explain what they're doing in plain English
  • They don't promise "guaranteed #1 rankings" (nobody can guarantee this)
  • They report monthly with the same metrics from Chapter 8
  • They don't own your GBP or citations — you do

What to avoid:

  • Agencies that won't tell you what they're doing
  • "SEO" companies that focus only on blog content and ignore GBP, citations, and reviews
  • Anyone who offers to buy you reviews
  • Contracts longer than 6 months with no performance benchmarks
  • Providers who create listings under their own accounts instead of yours
