The law firm marketing metrics that actually matter each month are the ones that connect marketing activity to revenue: cost per lead, cost per retained client, lead-to-consultation rate, and consultation-to-retention rate. Everything else is context at best and distraction at worst. If your monthly report leads with impressions, followers, or "reach," you're measuring effort, not results.

Most firms I talk to are drowning in data but starving for insight. They get a 15-page PDF from their agency every month, skim it, and file it away because nothing in it connects to the question they actually care about: is this working? A proper marketing scorecard strips away the noise and gives you five to seven numbers you can review in ten minutes that tell you whether to stay the course, adjust, or pull the plug.

## The Metrics That Belong on Your Scorecard

Here's what I recommend firms track monthly, at minimum:

**1. Total leads by source.** Not just total leads—leads broken out by where they came from. Google Ads, organic search, referrals, directories, social. If you can't attribute a lead to a source, your tracking is broken, and that's the first problem to fix.

**2. Cost per lead by source.** Your Google Ads might generate 40 leads a month, but if each one costs $400 and your directory listing generates 10 at $50 each, you need to know that. Aggregate cost-per-lead numbers hide the channels that are bleeding money.

**3. Lead-to-consultation rate.** How many of those leads actually get on the phone or walk through the door? If you're generating 100 leads and only 20 schedule a consultation, that's an 80% drop-off. The problem might be intake, response time, or lead quality—but you can't diagnose it if you're not measuring it.

**4. Consultation-to-retention rate.** Of the people who consult, how many sign? This is where marketing meets operations. A low close rate might not be a marketing problem at all—it could be pricing, the consultation experience, or fit.

**5. Cost per retained client.** This is the number. Total marketing spend divided by the number of clients actually retained. I've reviewed accounts where firms were paying $3,000 or more per retained client on channels they thought were "working" because the lead volume looked good.

**6. Revenue per client (or average case value).** Without this, cost per retained client is meaningless. Spending $2,000 to acquire a client on a $500 case is a disaster. Spending $2,000 to acquire a client on a $15,000 case is a different conversation entirely.

## Red Flags That Should Stop You Cold

Certain patterns in monthly data demand immediate attention. Not next quarter. Now.

**Lead volume is up but consultations are flat or down.** This almost always means lead quality has deteriorated. In accounts I've reviewed, this frequently traces back to broadened ad targeting, keyword drift, or a campaign that's attracting the wrong practice area inquiries.

**Cost per lead is climbing month over month with no change in strategy.** In competitive markets, this can signal increased auction pressure on paid channels. But it can also mean your landing pages have gone stale, your ad copy needs refreshing, or a competitor has entered the market aggressively.

**You can't connect a single retained client to a specific channel.** If your agency can't tell you which channels produced actual clients—not leads, clients—your tracking infrastructure has gaps. This is more common than it should be. Call tracking, CRM integration, and intake process discipline all have to work together.

**One channel dominates your entire pipeline.** Concentration risk applies to marketing just like it applies to a client roster. If 80% of your leads come from Google Ads, a policy change, account suspension, or budget disruption puts your entire pipeline at risk overnight.

## When to Pivot vs. When to Wait

This is where most firms either overreact or underreact. Both are expensive.

A general rule: paid channels should show directional data within 30 to 60 days. If you've been running Google Ads for 90 days with proper tracking and the cost per retained client is nowhere near viable, that's not a "give it more time" situation. That's a pivot.

SEO is different. Organic strategy legitimately needs six to twelve months to mature. But even there, you should see leading indicators—impressions trending up, keyword rankings moving, organic traffic growing—within the first few months. If everything is flat at month six, something is wrong with the strategy, not the timeline.

Content and social are the hardest to measure directly, and firms commonly treat them as "brand building" to avoid accountability. That's fine to a point, but if you've been publishing two blog posts a week for a year and organic traffic hasn't moved, the content strategy needs rethinking.

## Building the Actual Scorecard

Keep it simple. One page. I mean that literally.

A spreadsheet or simple dashboard with your five to seven core metrics, updated monthly, with a column for the prior month and a column for the three-month trend. Color-code it if you want—green, yellow, red—but the point is that any partner at the firm should be able to look at it for two minutes and know whether marketing is healthy.

Firms commonly make the mistake of letting their agency define the scorecard. The agency will naturally gravitate toward metrics that make their work look good. That's not dishonesty—it's human nature. You should define what you're measuring based on your business goals, and then ask the agency to report against those numbers.

The scorecard also forces a monthly conversation that has structure. Instead of "how's marketing going?" you're asking "why did cost per lead on Google Ads jump 30% this month?" and "what's driving the drop in consultation-to-retention rate?" Those are answerable questions that lead to action.

## Stop Guessing, Start Measuring

The firms that grow consistently aren't the ones spending the most on marketing. They're the ones who know exactly what each dollar produces and make decisions based on that data every single month. The scorecard is how you get there.
